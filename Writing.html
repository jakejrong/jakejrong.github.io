Part 1: Textbook

January 1, 1800. In the living room of the British governor’s mansion in London, the British regional governor Lord Phillips smoked a pipe. It was 2PM. Phillips was taking a half-hour break in his private quarters before boarding a stagecoach to Edinburgh for a meeting with the authorities of the region. Fifteen minutes later, after downing a pear and several sips of wine, Phillips walked out the front door and stepped onto the stagecoach, escorted by a dozen bodyguards armed with muskets, pistols and sabers. The driver was about to close the door when a messenger arrived. “Governor Phillips!” the panting aide cried. “The emperor has just been assassinated in North America. Parliament is currently in the process of appointing a successor, since there are no heirs by blood. As you are one of the most likely to succeed the emperor, be prepared. You may be called to Berlin right after returning from your Scottish journey.” Lord Phillips was taken aback, stunned at the abrupt, unexpected message. He could hardly believe what he had just heard. The 29-year old Emperor Edwards had just been killed? He had just been crowned two years earlier upon the death of his father. Now, on a visit to European Canada, Edwards was dead. He had been killed too young for any offspring, as he had not yet married. In this type of situation, Parliament was called upon to appoint a successor. The potential candidates permitted by law were any Member of Parliament, a regional governor, any lieutenant regional governor, or one of the three major generals in European high command. Although it was possible, no lieutenant governor had ever been appointed, nor was this an exception. The most prominent candidates in January of 1800 were the Representative from Flanders and the governors of Prussia, Great Britain, and Russia: Waller Peetsen, Hibb Hoffman, Lord Phillips and Gerppin Sokolov respectively. Phillips decided immediately to postpone his Scottish trip and proceed directly to Berlin. He wished to deliver a speech to Parliament to promote his campaign for the crown. The vote was taking place on March 15th. He needed to move fast if he hoped to receive the appointment.

The young Emperor Edwards had been in Quebec, the capital of European Canada on November 22nd, 1799. The region encompassing all of Northern North America had been under European control since 1765. However, that day, assassins hired by the native tribes to the south who detested European colonization of their lands were active in the capital city. One 20-year old recruit named Izzew was among the crowds overflowing the streets that day as Emperor Edwards paraded through at a slow pace. The monarch waved to his fans and carried a joyful expression. He had just finished an inspection of the troops and was now greeting the populace on his way to his hotel. Three days later Edwards was scheduled to board a ship returning to Europe. But the young emperor was to never see his homeland again. As Edwards, mounted on his horse, galloped past Izzew, the assassin, seeing his chance, pulled out a pistol and fired straight at the European ruler’s chest. The first shot missed, striking Edwards’ arm instead. But then Izzew fired another pistol and the round this time was fatal.  Edwards fell off his mount with blood flowing from his right arm and abdomen while his bodyguards tackled the assassin. The Emperor was quickly rushed to the hospital, but before he even reached the building it was clear that he was dead, killed instantly by the bullet that passed straight through his heart. Izzew, meanwhile, was put on trial for the assassination of Emperor Edwards and found guilty by a unanimous vote. He was sentenced to life imprisonment.

The slaying of the European monarch was to have far-reaching consequences beyond the appointment of a new emperor and the death of the previous. The North American native tribes who lived in modern-day USA and Mexico had united under one flag in 1760 under the singular goal of forcing out the European settlers. Canada had been lost, but the natives were determined not to relinquish any more. Their nation was dubbed the Dakota Confederacy by the Europeans due to the fact that their capital was in the Dakota region and their first chief was from there. The incumbent native chief was Blue Potato. An aggressive veteran campaigner, he was the third chief of the native tribes and had served in the ranks in all conflicts with the Europeans since 1772. At age 61, his old fighting spirit had not been put down. It was he who had opted to hire assassins to take out Emperor Edwards. Not surprisingly, the European ambassador to the native tribes, John Sterling, requested a meeting with him on December 1st. The native ambassador, Hollow Water, was the one who greeted the European. “Pardon me, my fellow diplomat, but where may I find your boss, Blue Potato?” asked Sterling as he entered the Native American capital building. “I’m afraid by custom you must speak with me, not the chief, unless your chief is present,” answered Hollow Water.

“Well, if that is what he demands then I suppose that is how it will have to be. I am simply here to ask whether your government had any role in this recent assassination of the emperor.”                                                                             “We are not involved in any way,” replied Hollow Water.                                                                                               “Is that an assurance and promise on your nation’s behalf?”                                                                                                         “Yes, I guarantee that to best of my government’s and my knowledge, we did not participate in the killing. Izzew was not in our employ. We did not have him under our radar before the assassination. We do not take responsibility.” Hollow Water responded.                                                                                                                                                           “I take your word for it that you are not responsible. But there will be an investigation, and if there is even a hint or trace that suggests that you had a part in this—”                                                                                                                           “Do not threaten me,” Hollow Water interrupted. “I’m aware of your intentions.”                                                               “Alright,” said the European ambassador. “You are fine people, and I trust you. If you are indeed guilty, admit it now. We will take some property and you will have to pay a fine of 15,000 mullions. If you do not, and we discover the truth, we will take military action against you. Izzew is to be interrogated tomorrow. What is your final decision, native?”                                                                                                                                                                                     “The native tribes of North America are not guilty of this heinous crime. We are innocent and you will find us to be in your so-called ‘investigation.’”                                                                                                                                      “Mark my words, ambassador. Your nation will suffer after Izzew spits out reality.” With that, Sterling turned on his heel and strolled out the door to his waiting carriage.

On January 25th, British governor Lord Phillips arrived in Berlin, the European capital. At 3PM he stepped into the Parliament building and delivered a speech to the representatives, asking that they vote for him, and speaking about all the reforms he would push Parliament to pass once he was appointed. At the end of the 20-minute address, the representatives cheered and bellowed their support for the governor. Phillips repeatedly asked Peetsen, the representative from Flanders, to drop out and endorse him, but Peetsen refused each time, despite saying in one of his own rallies in Vienna on the 29th that he agreed 75% which the positions of Governor Phillips. The other two candidates, Hoffman and Sokolov, had many differences with the British governor. Hoffman, however, posed hardly a challenge, since he ran only a low-level campaign in just his own province and Poland due to a lack of funds. Only Sokolov, the Russian governor, was a threat as he campaigned madly across Europe. By the start of March he had already made stops in Finland, Russia, Poland, France, Austria and Turkey, and Italy was next on the list. Within the same time, Phillips only visited Ireland, France, Spain and Italy. On March 2nd, he delivered an address in Athens at the foot of the Parthenon. The British governor then immediately made for Berlin to view the voting in process.

The governor-general of European Canada was furious. He had been in deep conference in the dining hall of the Governor-General’s Mansion with his advisors, generals, and the leaders of the Callen Commission, named after its chairman, Martin Callen, the Canadian solicitor general. The governor-general, Girard de Bouqonet, asked Callen about the result of two months of investigation by the commission into the assassination of Emperor Edwards. Callen braced himself for an uproar as he replied: “It was the Indians, your honor.”                                                           Bouqonet threw his wineglass on the tiled floor, and before it even broke into pieces with an alarming sound he had already begun screaming. “How could it have been the Indians?! I was assured by Sterling that they were innocent! It must be some radical group or supporters of Sokolov or Phillips. Why, I’ll have both of them arrested during the coronation ceremony! I can make it just in time if I send the message right away—”                                                                 “No, sir,” interrupted Callen. “The culprit is without a doubt Blue Potato. Izzew confirmed that during the interrogation.”                                                                                                                                                                   “He could very well be lying,” responded Bouqonet.                                                                                                             “The prisoner had a note on him from Blue Potato himself,” stated Callen as he passed the small piece of parchment to the governor-general.                                                                                                                                                                Bouqonet immediately called his translator over, and after two minutes of whispered conversing he stood up and pounded his fist on the table. General Collingwood, the most prominent general in Canada, had his wine spill into his lap. Bouqonet shouted angrily, “Well, well. So the natives are liars, thinking they can escape this predicament. We’ll see about that. Martin, have two armies mobilized by Friday. I want to force them to pay a fee double what Sterling demanded for this crime. We will also be taking hostages, and if the ransom is not paid they shall be executed. ”                                                                                                                                                                       “Yes, your honor,” Callen replied as he scurried away to carry out the order.

On March 15th, Governor Phillips and Sokolov were both sitting in a high chamber with great anxiousness as they watched the parliamentary voting process. Peetsen, the representative from Flanders, was sitting below on the first story. Hoffman had not bothered to attend, knowing he only had a chance of winning his home region of Prussia. Finally, at 5:30PM, Prime Minister Joppel Toth announced that the voting had finished and counting had commenced. A half-hour later, after an official count and re-count, Toth stepped onto the podium again and declared the winner. “I hereby decree that Governor Lord Phillips of Great Britain is our new emperor of Europe!” bellowed Toth. Phillips jumped in the air with joy and hugged and shook hands with his campaign managers and advisors, also sitting the chamber with him. Cheers sprang up across the hall. Sokolov conceded the election to Phillips later that day, as did Peetsen. Hoffman was waiting outside the Parliament building and happily greeted all three candidates. He had indeed only secured the votes of the Prussian representatives. The Prussian governor invited the new emperor-elect to a celebration dinner which Phillips gladly accepted. Toth offered Phillips the opportunity to choose his own coronation date, which the British governor set for May 8th.

The Dakota Confederacy was well-prepared for an invasion by the Europeans. Blue Potato had been expecting confrontation with Bouqonet ever since the assassination. He hoped to score a few decisive early victories which would convince other nations to join the war against Europe. Africa, after all, also housed European colonies and had fought many earlier wars against Europe. Their relations with the Europeans were no better than that of the Native Americans. Blue Potato expected an attack by sea against the East Coast, since Canada only had sufficient troops for self-defense. At best Bouqonet could hope to coordinate an operation with the European commanders on the East Coast. To no one’s surprise, Blue Potato received an ultimatum on March 17th from Bouqonet:                        “We require 30,000 mullions and eighty hostages if you are to be forgiven for the assassination of Edwards. Agree by April 1st or there will be consequences. You must pay the fee and hand over the prisoners by the end of the year.” Blue Potato, upon reading the message, tore up the slip of paper. He would never answer Bouqonet’s ultimatum.  Instead, he ordered his best generals, Bone Wrangler and Shark Bull to lead their own separate armies of 120,000 each towards the East Coast and prepare defensive fortifications. 25,000 troops would be guarding the Canadian coast in the event of an invasion by land.

At 8AM on April 10th, 1799, Governor-general Girard de Bouqonet of Canada chowed down on a traditional English breakfast of eggs, sausages, mushrooms, baked beans, hash browns and a tomato slice, served with a tall glass of brandy. He ate on the porch of his mansion, awaiting word from Ambassador John Sterling as to whether the natives had complied with the offered terms. Around nine, Bernard Pierre, Bouqonet’s senior advisor, received a written message from Sterling. It informed Bouqonet that the natives had rejected the ultimatum and admitted to having assassinated Emperor Edwards. The governor-general was not caught by surprised, having expected such a response the entire time. “Mr. Pierre, I have something to ask of you,” he requested to his aide.                                      “Yes, Governor.”                                                                                                                                                                          “Tell Phillips that we have a situation on our hands. An urgent one that will require the full force of the European military. High command needs to be called in.”                                                                                                                          “Yes sir, right away.”                                                                                                                                                    

Governor Phillips had become Emperor Phillips following an elaborate coronation ceremony on May 8th. His wife, Sophia Meyer, became the new Empress. Phillips had announced in his campaign speeches that he would work on behalf of the poor and downtrodden peasants. He wished to create a larger middle class and smaller low and high classes. The first step was to increase taxes on the rich by 15% and decrease taxes on the impoverished by 15%. However, the incumbent justice minister refused to carry out such an order. As a result, Phillips replaced him with, to everyone’s surprise, Gerppin Sokolov, the Russian governor. Phillips personally swore him into his new position on May 20th. His main reason for doing so was to promote better relations between the two men. He believed the Russian to be an energetic and good-willed fellow, and thought he would serve as a fine gentleman in the government, seeing how energetically he had campaigned for emperor. Unfortunately, moments after the ceremony Phillips was whisked away to Paris to discuss major developments in North America.

Blue Potato had secretly ordered his best commander, Blood Drawer, to attack Fort Toronto just north of Lake Ontario. Blood Drawer had rowed an army of 80,000 men across the Great Lakes by cover of darkness on the night of April 22nd. By 5AM the following morning, the fortress was surrounded by Native American warriors. Upon hearing the good news that Blood Drawer had fulfilled his task without being detected, Blue Potato himself came to command the army personally. The chief arrived a month later on May 21st. He immediately held a council of war outside his tent in the main camp.  “So, my subordinates, what is the best way to deal with this situation?”                                     “Well, chief,” answered Pollum Hiawatha, another Native commander, “our army has been divided and is stretched to the limit. Over 300,000 troops are stationed either here or on the East coast. Reserves are minimal and number around 20,000. I would suggest we capture Toronto as soon as possible. Remember, Chief. Our goal is to score decisive victories over the Europeans, convincing Africa to join our side. Keep that in mind.”                                                   “I like the sound of Pollum’s idea. Blood Drawer, what are your plans?”                                                                                    “Hiawatha’s strategy certainly sounds reasonable. We could storm the castle, in this case Fort Toronto, and secure the fortress. Our spies report that it holds only 10,000 defenders in all, along with a mere 29 artillery pieces. That would certainly strike fear into the hearts of the Canadians. It would put them on alert. It would worry and demoralize Bouqonet and his men. Chief, what is the main objective again? To arouse Africa, currently sleeping, to join our side? And then run the Europeans out of Canada?”                                                                                               “Yes, that is the primary objective we base our plans on, Blood Drawer,” responded Blue Potato.                                             “That’s what I thought,” continued Blood Drawer. “But I see a problem. The Europeans will arrive in force before long. That means thousands of troops, perhaps even as high as 400,000 men. That’s well over our full military strength. Sure, the Africans can support us. But they are just as far from our shores as the Europeans are. They can’t possibly help us over here. Why, it’ll take them a year or two just to push the Europeans out of their continent. That’s hard enough. I can’t imagine them being able to force the Europeans out of ours.”                                                      “My dear general,” said Blue Potato calmly, “you are an upstart young fellow. You have forgotten that the war is not restricted to this zone. Why, the Africans can help us by opening a second front. And that means arousing a conflict in the Mediterranean. That’ll keep the Europeans occupied and make thing easier on our front. It’ll ease our forces and put strain on the Europeans’. That shall help us far more than sending their men way over here. Don’t you think so, lad?”                                                                                                                                                                                       “That’s it! What a brilliant idea, Chief.”                                                                                                                                  “That’s why I’m the head, Blood Drawer. I let youngsters like you handle the negotiations and strategy, and we’ll have a civil war on our hands in no time.”                                                                                                                             “So what’s the final decision, Chief?” asked Hiawatha.                                                                                                           “Storm the gates, Blood Drawer. This fortress will be ours within the next 48 hours, or I resign as chief of the Native tribes.”            

Bouqonet was once again in a state of outrage. Not only had those bastardly natives refused his terms; they had attacked a major fort on the border! The governor-general summoned Bernard Pierre, his senior advisor. “Bernard. I want four armies of ours on the East Coast by mid-August. Tell Phillips that these Native Americans will be punished beyond their wildest dreams if he approves.”                                                                                                                         “Yes, Governor-general, right away.”                                                     

Fort Toronto surrendered on May 23rd. Blood Drawer’s troops had fired hundreds of arrows into the city, then followed up the barrage with a mass assault involving 50,000 men. Thousands of native soldiers stormed up ladders and scaled the walls. Few of Blood Drawer’s troops wore armor, instead it was animal skins, intricate feathered headwear, and felt or leather shoes. Each man carried an axe, sword or dagger. The European defenders launched several volleys of musket fire at the oncoming warriors, but they were overwhelmed and overcome quickly. The fort’s commander, James Willabe, committed suicide by shooting himself in the head with his revolver. His second-in-command, rather than continue the fighting to the end, raised the white flag and nearly 6,000 European troops dropped their arms with him. Blood Drawer had been dubbed his name due to his policy of “no mercy” with prisoners, but this time, with the chief present, he consulted Blue Potato for permission to slaughter the Europeans. In addition to the lot of troops there were as many as 15,000 civilians captured by his army. “Chief, we have about 20,000 enemies rounded up in the courtyard under close watch, civilian and soldier. What would you like me to do with them, your honor?”                                                                                                                                                               “Keep them in custody and feed each one a meal a day.  That’ll ground their strength and prevent them from revolting and trying to escape. We can use them as hostages. If the Europeans leave our land forever, they can have their people back.”                                                                                                                                                               “Good thinking, chief. But no maltreatment?”                                                                                                                           “No. Just feed them less. Pollum,” demanded Blue Potato, “you are to be the commander solely in charge of guarding these prisoners. If one makes a getaway and succeeds, you will be receiving 80 lashes and a demotion. Is that clear?”                                                                                                                                                                               “Yes sir. I will keep my eye on them day and night.”                                                                                                                “Do a good job with that, and you may get a more ‘active’ assignment like Bone Wrangler, Shark Bull, and why, even my right-hand man Blood Drawer here. Blood Drawer, I need you to collect all operation weapons that the enemy left behind. Any firearms would be especially beneficial, along with ammunition. While our main armies are waiting on the coast, you can take your army of 70,000 and attack the small village of Hamilton, about 20 miles from Fort Toronto. If you move fast we can pull off another swift victory and capture more hostages.”                              “Yes, Chief. I will do my best. Hopefully we can convince the Africans to join the war before long.”                                

Emperor Phillips called an emergency meeting of Parliament as soon as he was informed of the native attack. On June 30th, 1800, the day before Parliament was scheduled to convene, another message arrived from Bouqonet: Fort Toronto has fallen. The Emperor shook his head in disbelief, but it was obvious that the natives had been lying. Blue Potato had been the cause of the assassination. The slaying of the European monarch was one of the highest crimes one could commit. The following day, after lunch, Parliament gathered in the Berlin Parliament building.  Justice Minister Gerppin Sokolov began with a 10-minute address declaring that the Dakota Confederacy had committed a very heinous sin by shooting former emperor Edwards. Finally, he rose from the podium and handed it over to Phillips. The new emperor asked in a 5-minute speech for Parliament to declare war on the natives.  “Members of Parliament, representatives, Prime Minister Toth, Committee chairmen, ministers, I thank you for coming here today. Justice Minister Sokolov just delivered to us the very state of the crime committed by Native Chief Blue Potato and his followers. They make up the Dakota Confederacy. They are upset over our colonizing their lands. Well, I say, it is perfectly justified for us to take territory which they themselves hardly use! Why, here in Europe our cities are full of rubbish and filth. We need space to expand! We are too crowded here! It is certainly no problem for the natives to move south when they as an entire nation have a population scarcely  of ours. We asked them to carry their goods with them and go. Their reasoning for killing our emperor is ‘retribution’. Well, I say, if they can use that, we can use that! Governor-general Bouqonet demands we send four armies to make those native fools pay. I say, we send eight, no, ten armies and make them pay even more! We are clearly the more civilized and advanced nation. I was just told recently that Fort Toronto has fallen to the natives. They hold thousands of our brothers and sisters captive. If they slaughter them, we will slaughter millions of theirs. We will punish the Dakota Confederacy beyond anything it can dream up. If they burn our cities, fortresses and churches to the ground, we will burn theirs to the point where it will never burn again! I hereby ask you, members of Parliament, to declare a state of war against the empire of Blue Potato and his evil supporters in the Dakota Confederacy.” Ending his speech with a bow, Emperor Phillips walked off the stage and boarded a stagecoach headed for Paris. Prime Minister Toth immediately quieted the cheering and hollering representatives and commenced voting. The following morning, Sokolov himself stepped onto the podium and announced that the result had been 992-7 in favor of war. One representative had not attended the previous day’s session due to family-related issues. Waller Peetsen, the representative from Flanders, immediately demanded to know who had voted against a declaration of war. Sokolov refused to say, strongly angering Peetsen who requested over and over again. Eventually he took off his shoe and was preparing to hurl it at the justice minister when two Parliamentary guards tackled him. He was led out by three members of the security forces. Prime Minister Toth stepped onto the stage; Sokolov, taking the hint, promptly left the building. Toth officially concluded the session of Parliament and declared that “the Dakota Confederacy will be under our heel by 1802”.

Three weeks after the declaration of war against the natives, Emperor Phillips arrived in Paris on July 22nd to discuss strategy with the European high command. The three major generals at the time were Gummen, Bellefleur and Weber, the third also being the governor of Austria. Phillips told his commanders during the 3-hour conference that he wanted seven armies, in all 350,000 men, to be landed on the East Coast of North America, along with cavalry and artillery supplements. Coordinating operations with General Collingwood, the Canadian military chief, the European forces would attack the natives from north and east. They would capture the capital of the Dakota Confederacy, Eropet, in the Dakotas, and that would deal a fatal blow leading to the eventual collapse of the nation with European troops closing in. It looked like a brilliant plan on paper devised by Phillips and presented to his commanders. The emperor chose for Bellefleur and Gummen to lead the massive force, which would be split into two armies of 175,000. Weber had not been selected since a regional governor could not be out of the continent for over six months at a time by law. The formidable invasion force gathered on the coast of Spain over the course of August, and on September 3rd it set sail aboard an incredible armada of 1,700 ships for North America. The fleet was under the command of European vice admiral Herbert Vincent. Bellefleur and Gummen were staying onboard the flagship Conquistador with him. In addition to the flagship, there were 52 other large warships escorting the 1,700 transports, along with 8 frigates. About half of the vessels belonged to the British Royal Navy, 12 to the French and 18 to the Spanish.

Bouqonet was not feeling well. He had gotten enraged again upon hearing that Fort Toronto had surrendered. His mood only worsened when Pierre told him about the hostages. Bouqonet had immediately ordered Collingwood to lead two Prussian armies stationed in Canada against the natives, but then a message from Phillips arrived. It told him to wait until a reinforcing army of 350,000 arrived. When that happened, he would receive further instruction. Bouqonet relayed the order to Collingwood. Pierre informed him several days later that the small town of Hamilton had also been captured by Blood Drawer. It was too much for the Canadian governor-general to sit back and have his commanders remain idle while innocent civilians were being held captive and killed. On August 2nd, he gave Collingwood the order to subdue this major threat to the peace and stability of European Canada.

Hamilton had a population of approximately 9,000. Of that number, 1,500 men between the ages of 20-55 belonged to the local militia. However, they were caught completely off guard by Blood Drawer’s native cavalry, which stormed into the village at 4AM on the morning of June 8th, 1800. Within an hour, 382 militiamen had been slaughtered. Their captain surrendered the town around 6 to prevent further bloodshed. Blood Drawer himself rode in at noon, inspecting the scene. The town was practically untouched. He had lost 17 horsemen and another 29 were wounded. He immediately sent a messenger to Blue Potato, still in Fort Toronto, to tell him the news.

With Hamilton also taken, Blue Potato highly expected Bouqonet to send an army against him. His spies alerted him on August 15th that a Prussian advance guard had been spotted 25 miles north of Fort Toronto. Unfortunately, the size of the enemy force remained uncertain. Blue Potato decided that Blood Drawer could handle the situation himself. He would merely supply him with an additional 20 pieces of artillery.

Collingwood was, unfortunately, an inexperienced commander. He had decided to attack the natives rather than wait them out, as he should have. Blood Drawer was waiting for him atop Frankenheimer hill. The native commander had set up his headquarters atop the hill, along with the 20 heavy guns had received: 10 9-pounders and 10 12-pounders. Collingwood had with him no artillery, having been told by Bouqonet that the natives had hardly any and would not risk losing what little they possessed so early in the campaign. The Dakota Confederacy did indeed have very few artillery pieces; only 50 cannon in all. They had either been captured from the Europeans in earlier battles or gained from trading with Dutch settlers before hostilities had ensued. But Blue Potato staked 40% of it on this one battle. Not only did the natives hold few cannon, they were in low supply of firearms as well. They could only field 22,000 troops armed with any type of gun. The rest had to make do with spears, swords, axes and shields. Most of the ranged units still used either bows and arrows or crossbows. The technological advantage was what gave the Europeans under Collingwood a morale boost. Canada at this time could raise only a maximum of 150,000 troops, excluding militia. It was that way because Bouqonet, as well as the generals in European high command, believed this immense difference in technology would make each European soldier equivalent to ten natives. Therefore, there was no need for extra funds being spent on what general Bellefleur called “petty fears”. The Europeans would pay for this major mistake in the oncoming battle.

Collingwood started off by sending his infantry, 20,000 musket-armed troops. They marched in columns across the open grassy plain and were hit by continuous artillery barrages. Blood Drawer had positioned his men just behind the hill, keeping them out of sight. Once the European infantry had gotten within 500 feet of the hill, he sent them up at a charge. Collingwood’s men were caught unawares and struggled to get into line. But before a shot could be fired, the native warriors had made contact. Thousands of sabers, swords, cutlasses, axes, spears, hatchets and clubs stabbed, jabbed, tore and ripped at the flesh and body of the European troops. The muskets the Europeans carried held bayonets, but they were ineffective compared to the close-combat weaponry the natives used. Two hours later, after rough and bloody fighting, Collingwood’s columns had routed and were in full retreat. The European commander threw in the elite Prussian foot guard reserve of 5,000, as well as the cavalry, to save the day, but they were similarly hit hard by Blood Drawer’s artillery. Blood Drawer sent his own cavalry against the foot guards, who formed square and repelled three charges. Eventually, the cannons ran out of ammunition. The European cavalry had a close fight with the native infantry, but in the end the foot guards routed and the battle was won for the natives. Blood Drawer’s cavalry had suffered the heaviest losses: around 7,000 horsemen killed or wounded. The combat infantry had lost 4,000 men. Collingwood retreated back to Fort Ottawa, leaving 18,000 European dead on the field. 23,000 troops had been wounded. It was a horrific defeat for the Europeans.

Blue Potato was ecstatic upon hearing of the decisive victory scored by Blood Drawer. Immediately, he ordered the news relayed to Africa. Unfortunately, dampening this report was a message from Bone Wrangler. The European invasion force was bound to arrive within the next two weeks. The Dakota Confederacy hardly possessed a navy. In 1800, it totaled 4 warships stationed on the East Coast and 250 war canoes along with 10 frigates positioned on the Great Lakes. The war canoes were tiny compared to the gigantic ships-of-the-line the Europeans sailed. They could hold a maximum of 10 men and required paddling to move. Knowing that there was no chance of stopping the European invasion fleet, Blue Potato ordered the navy to retreat back to harbor. As it was already late September, the chief expected the Europeans to try and defeat his army on land immediately, then make winter quarters in preparation for intense fighting in the spring. Meanwhile, more reinforcements would arrive through the established beachhead. This would tip the scales against the natives, and eventually, attacked on two sides and with overwhelming enemy numbers, Blue Potato would be crushed and forced to surrender. To prevent this, the chief realized he had only one option: keep the Europeans in the water. He must not allow them to establish a beachhead. In his response to Bone Wrangler, Blue Potato urged his general to thoroughly fortify the Atlantic waterfront. Not one enemy soldier should breach their defenses.

Bouqonet had lost two valuable, well-trained, well-equipped, irreplaceable Prussian divisions. High command would be furious if they found out. The governor-general knew that to salvage his reputation and prestige, he needed to score a similar victory over Blue Potato. After hearing the horrific news on the 20th of September, he had considered sacking Collingwood, but there were no better European commanders in Canada, only ones of equal or lesser competence. The best generals were either in Europe or on their way to North America aboard the invasion armada. It would be at least another two weeks before their arrival. In the meantime, with the capital of Quebec threatened, Bouqonet decided to call up the national militia to defend the city. By September 25th, in addition to the regular garrison of 15,000, thirty-thousand militia were raised from the surrounding region and began training exercises.

Admiral Vincent’s lookout aboard the European flagship Conquistador spotted land at 4PM on November 4th, 1800. After the admiral verified the claim, there were mass celebrations aboard ship. The entire fleet was in a joyous state for the whole of the evening. On the Conquistador, barrels of beer and wine were rolled out and sailors drank to their heart’s content. Humongous trays of splendid food were brought out, and dining went on until nine that night. Gummen and Bellefleur, the senior commanders aboard, although invited to the celebratory supper by Admiral Vincent, did not attend. Instead a servant brought their dinner to them and they ate in Gummen’s quarters while in deep discussion over what they should do. After two hours of debate, the two generals finally decided on waiting for reports from the lookout. Gummen, witnessing the failure from the deck of the Conquistador, was thoroughly disappointed. With the attack in shambles, there was nothing left to do but retreat. In all, the two unsuccessful assaults had cost nearly 10,000 European dead or wounded. It was a terrible price for virtually no gain. Shark Bull, on the other hand, was satisfied. Just as Gummen had feared, Blood Drawer’s scouts neared in on Fort Ottawa on March 24th and saw the European army on the move along the road to Fort Toronto. Emperor Phillips was working in his Berlin Parliamentary office on June 3rd, 1801. The following morning, Bone Wrangler began halfway through breakfast. “Now, Chief, while you were away, in mid-summer Blood Drawer decided to launch an attack on Sault St. Marie, which is one of the westernmost Canadian settlements. So he sent General Hiawatha on a mission to capture the town, which our spies believed held a force and population much lower than the force we intended to send against it. The attack was a success, and although we sustained losses, Mr. Hiawatha was able to take the city. The general idea behind Blood Drawer’s strategy was to capture Sault St. Marie, and then move east from there against Sudbury, Ottawa, Montréal, and eventually Québec. I think we can ascertain that after Québec is seized, there is little that will stand in the way of us forcing the Europeans out of Canada. What do you have to comment on anything of relevance, Chief?”                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          “Seems like a decent plan to me,” replied Blue Potato as he took a bite of acorn bread. “Kind of like a ‘city-hopping’ theme. The only problem is…I’m not sure how we will force the Europeans to surrender their cities to us. A siege won’t do it, Bone Wrangler. Fort Ottawa is well protected. It probably holds at least 30,000 men, with plenty of artillery, ammunition and food stocks. It can be easily resupplied by the other large settlements to the north.”                                                                    “Don’t worry, Chief. We have a way around this issue. Blood Drawer says that he’ll use our heavy artillery to pound their ways to smithereens, then make our way in using brute force. Have no fear, Chief; there shall not be any imperfections. He has even planned out a landing of 20,000 mounted troops using the navy. This force will sail up Lake Ontario, land just south of Fort Ottawa and in the dead of night, and surprise the enemy defenders by scaling their walls from the rear! It’s a foolproof plan that’s bound to succeed!”                                                                                                    “Oh, I wouldn’t get so excited about this scheme just yet. We have had too many sequential victories and practically no losses. The chances of that pattern reversing itself increase after each triumph.”

Elias Gustafson stood watching from a sofa in the lobby as servants carried his personal items and furniture from a wagon to his new office on the 2nd floor of the European field headquarters in Quebec. It was January 3rd, 1802. Governor-general Girard de Bouqonet had just sworn him in as the new head of Canadian armed forces two days ago. It was now 4PM. Gustafson had met with his advisors that morning. He had officially announced to them that he was removing Collingwood’s invasion of Mexico from the agenda. Instead, he would focus on the navy, in order to achieve dominance on the Great Lakes. That way, for the natives to reinforce their base, Fort Toronto, they would have to go by land, either to the west, beyond Lake Superior, cross the pass which would be guarded by European forces, or move overland at Montréal, also a heavily defended European border. From there, the plan was to cut off Fort Toronto, and hopefully the high-positioned native leaders as well, most importantly Blue Potato. The Canadian genera’s staff had happily approved the plan. The navy was scheduled to initiate the blockade operation, codenamed Venus, on March 20th, 1802, using a total of 80 warships. Gustafson had sent the details of the plan off to high command in Paris for approval. In the meantime, he would do what Collingwood had decided not to and go on a regional tour to try and rally up support. The goal was the same as Collingwood had stated: Encourage as many males, between the ages of 20-55, preferably on the younger end, to enlist for the coming year. In fact, to promote the tour, one of Gustafson’s first acts in office was to abolish the mandatory draft, which Collingwood had begun enforcing the previous fall. The tour was scheduled to last from January 10th to February 25th, and cover all the major cities in southern Canada, as well as touch some of the outskirts to draw in as many fresh recruits as possible. Québec, Montréal, Fort Ottawa, Sudbury, Fort Albany and its surroundings, and even cities as distant as Shefferville, Goose Bay and St. Anthony on the Atlantic Coast would be reached. After rallying in Northern Canada, Gustafson hoped to make the most of his trip by taking the slow route back to Québec, and taking time to make an appearance in as many towns as he could along the St. Lawrence. The last settlement he wished to visit was a small, obscure village known as Pine Dock, just 25 miles northeast of Québec. The journey could take months, and as a result Gustafson did not feel confident that he could wrap things up by the end of February, in time to lead the attack against Fort Toronto in the spring. In such a scenario, which was highly likely, he had already picked out the interim commander-in-chief of the operation, Maxim Ivanov, the newly arrived Russian general, who would hold the position until Gustafson returned.

At this point, both sides had devised plans of action for the upcoming spring season: European and native. It was a matter of who took the initiative and attacked first. Unfortunately, Blue Potato learned on January 28th that the invasion planned by Blood Drawer was scheduled to begin in mid-April. Although the chief did not know it at the time, that one-month difference would cost his nation dearly.

Just as planned, at 5AM on March 20th, the 80 warships took off from Québec under the command of Vice Admiral Herbert Vincent. Gustafson’s plan had been approved by high command, which had surprisingly voyaged over from Europe and arrived at the beginning of March, due to Emperor Phillips’ desire to command the war personally. He had brought with him an elite group of men who he hoped would allow this offensive to be the one that would finally tip the scales back in European favor. This highly trained, top-ranking group included:

·       Franz Müller and his elite Austrian jägers

·       John Sterling, ambassador to the Dakota Confederacy

·       William Davis and his Scottish royal guards (responsible for protection of the Scottish royal family and the regional governor)

·       Felix Fusch (commissioner of the Berlin Police Department) and 2,000 burly Berlin police officers

·       Major General Alexander Weber, as his term as Austrian governor had ended

·       Paul Saminov and the Russian Imperial Guard

·       Dean Michel and the feared French artillery batteries

·       Sophia Meyer, Phillips’ wife, for moral support                                           

“With these men on our side,” proclaimed Emperor Phillips, showing General Gustafson, freshly returned from his tour, the elites in a traditional line-up, “those natives don’t stand a chance!”                                                                                                    “I wouldn’t say that just yet. Those natives can be pretty crafty,” replied Gustafson.                                                                                                                 “Nonsense, Elias. We will most certainly win now. The navy is already executing your brilliantly planned operation. Why, high command has even come to personally observe the upcoming battle. Surely the presence of our top generals, my wife, these elite units and me are a hefty morale boost. How many men we got, Elias?”                                              “We have 60,000 line infantry and skirmishers, the elite units, numbering 12,000, at least 40,000 sailors aboard 80 warships, and 700 more transports, along with 150 artillery pieces. That makes a combined total of 112,000 soldiers, but the only ones that matter to us are the 100,000 men on land. The navy is under the control of –”                                                                                                   “Yes, I know who our vice admiral is, Elias. You needn’t tell me that. Now, are our troops already heading towards the predestinated destination or not? I’m eager to follow along and watch the action.”                                                                                                                                                                             “Of course, your highness. Would you like to board your flagship now and sail for Fort Toronto, sir?”                                          “I’d be delighted, Elias. But you need to command your troops, so head on ahead of me, quickly, while I take the leisurely route there. Server! I would like some freshly squeezed grape juice, please. There’s nothing like enjoying a chilled, refreshing drink as you cruise down the St. Lawrence, viewing a major battle in style.”

The native generals were taken completely by surprise. Just as Gustafson had hoped, Blue Potato, Blood Drawer and Pollum Hiawatha were caught trapped in the Fort Toronto landmass, although he did not know this. The European navy under Vincent moved in with speed and by April 4th the blockade was in place. Lakes Huron, Erie and Ontario were now all swarming with European warships. The transports carried troops to various locations where they blocked up any potential escape routes. The land bridges connecting the landmass to the Dakota town of Detrito and Fort Ottawa were now guarded by thousands of European troops, who began construction of watchtowers and defensive walls. Next, the navy shelled the coastal town of Hamilton, bombarding it until it was leveled and burning. Upon finishing this, Vice Admiral Herbert Vincent ordered 4,000 of his sailors forward into the ruins to take any survivors, if either nobles or in reasonable shape, prisoner. Everyone else was to be executed. While watching the town slowly go up in flames, Emperor Phillips arrived in his flagship, the Royal Oak. “Hello there, Admiral!” he called from the deck of his ship. “Good to see you, your highness. How are you doing? Would you care to join me in watching the destruction of our enemy’s village? I just had our ships pound it to the point of disintegration.”                                                                    “Good to hear, good to hear, Herbert. You wouldn’t happen to have any fine Chenin Blanc, would you, Admiral?”                          “As a matter of fact, I have a lovely assortment of vintage wine in the cargo, sir. Would you like a peek?”                                               “I would be delighted, Herbert, to enjoy a few sips with you while we watch this splendid sight.”                                                             

As the European commanders were rejoicing at the magnitude of their surprise attack, the troops on the ground were approaching their destinations. The navy had already landed 15,000 veterans on the overland route connecting Fort Toronto to Detrito, and by the morning of April 4th walls were being erected to keep anyone from going in or out. Gustafson planned to send another 5,000 new recruits in as reinforcement within the next two weeks. The same was occurring on the thin pass just north of Lake Ontario, but that was already European territory. The wall therefore was only one-sided, but still 12,000 troops patrolled it. The entire operation was proceeding just as planned. Gustafson, sitting in the Northumberland, now his official personal ship, viewed the horrific fire consuming Hamilton just a few thousand feet from where the emperor and Vincent were witnessing the same scene. He had directed the troops blocking the two overland escape routes from Fort Toronto, of which both groups were now in place. On the 8th he would be in Fort Ottawa, and from there personally lead an army of 50,000, in addition to the elite faction headed by Emperor Phillips, against Toronto. The final goal was capture of the top native commanders, but even now European high command was still uncertain as to whether Blue Potato, the enemy chief, was in the fortress.

The native head himself was in a rare state of being. He had been laying on his bed for the past five days and nights without eating, and only taking occasional sips of water and tea. Blue Potato was panic-stricken and beyond distressed by the current situation. He was trapped in Fort Toronto, with only 20,000 men and two generals. There was no way to communicate with the outside world due to the enemy blockade. His own navy was rubbish in comparison to the Europeans’. The circumstances appeared excessively bleak. How could this be resolved? Blue Potato had already spoken with Hiawatha and Blood Drawer in 2 emergency councils of war, but the two commanders had no solutions of their own. As a result, the defenders’ morale sank significantly. Blue Potato began to request paper, ink and a quill pen in addition to his daily water and tea, so that he could start drafting his papers of surrender to Emperor Phillips.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         

Around 9AM on April 8th, 1802, Gustafson’s “Trounce of Triumph” began. For half an hour, he spoke to his army, hoping that it would motivate the men and give them maximum morale. The only order of the day before had been to get a good night’s sleep, so everyone would be in prime physical condition. Foolishly, Gustafson forgot to send out scouts, and his fellow commanders, not wanting to spoil the moment by voicing an error, did not point out the mistake to him. As a result, the columns were startled a day later when their lines were raked by sniper fire coming from within the woodland on both sides. Gustafson had 3,000 of his troops scare them away, first by firing several volleys into the forest and then chasing the snipers until they fled. After the incident, which had caused 800 casualties and undid all that Gustafson’s speech had done, the march continued peacefully. However, this was what only what the general was told. Unaware to the rest of the force, 2,000 native light horsemen had cut off Dean Michel’s elite artillery batteries, which had fallen behind due to their slow pace, from the main body. The cavalry easily slaughtered the helpless artillerymen, armed with only daggers and sabers, and cut the unit to pieces. Michel himself managed to flee back to Fort Ottawa, but he was one of only 3 survivors. No one was able to inform Gustafson about what had happened. The horsemen, meanwhile, suffering just 4 men wounded and one horse killed, captured 40 guns of the best French artillery, as well as many rounds of ammunition. Unfortunately, they did come with operators, but the cavalry had been told to exterminate them, as it was not believed that they would be willing to fire on their comrades.

On April 15th, Gustafson’ army of Southern Canada, still numbering nearly 100,000 men, reached Fort Toronto. Emperor Phillips himself had left the Royal Oak the previous day to witness this dramatic battle in person. His wife, the empress Sophia Meyer, was also present. It was not until 3PM, though, with the European troops already in action fighting the defenders that Maxim Ivanov, one of Gustafson’s top-ranking subordinates, realized that Dean Michel’s heavy artillery was missing. He immediately reported his finding to Gustafson. The general, suddenly alarmed, sent a messenger to Québec, requesting some heavy guns. Lacking artillery, the only way Gustafson could win the siege was by mass assault. The following morning, the sleepy native sentries watching the plains ahead of them were startled by a cascade of bullets. Seconds later, they were all dead. Franz Müller’s deadly jägers had struck again. The guards dead, 9,000 European infantry began to scale the wall using ladders, but were met by vicious Dakota warriors armed with heavy axes and swords. The close combat atop the wall continued fiercely into the afternoon. After a pause in the fighting for dinner around 7PM, Gustafson took a gamble and sent the Scottish royal guards, led by their captain, William Davis, along with 20,000 line infantry. The European troops slowly marched up to the base of the wall, the normal infantry’s morale boosted by the presence of the Scottish elites. However, the feeling did not last. Just as the royal guards reached the top of the wall, they were lit aflame. The natives had prepared mini-lanterns, full of highly flammable oils. The elite soldiers panicked and many fell right off the wall to their deaths. It was a horrific sight. The line infantry behind them, frightened, routed and fled back to the safety of their camp.

There were no more assaults for the next 4 days. On April 22nd, Gustafson called a council of war aboard the largest ship in the fleet, the 125-gun Prince of Orange. Alexander Weber and Maxim Ivanov, the two major generals present, delivered their beliefs to Gustafson. “The situation grows worse by day,” said Weber. “We’ve lost all our artillery to the natives. I myself took a battalion of light cavalry to investigate, and we discovered the corpses of all of Dean Michel’s artillerymen. However, every last gun was gone, and so was the ammunition. Since horse imprints were rampant over the field, I have reason to believe that the natives cut off the artillery column and massacred it, then made off with some of our finest cannonry! Chances are, they’ll begin using our own guns against us, once they can figure out how to fire them. Assault after assault on those walls has proven unsuccessful. What can we do? I recommend we prepare a battering ram and try to take down that main gate. The Scottish royal guards have suffered heavy losses, and so we’re sending them home. The Russian Imperials and the Berlin police officers remain fit for battle, but I would not commit them just yet. Morale is growing lower and lower. I say while we wait for the replacement artillery to arrive, we try out this battering ram. Maxim, your thoughts?”                                                                     “Well, I would suggest that we send in some spies to figure out exactly what the enemy is thinking. No doubt they want to confront and defeat us before our substitute cannons can be put to use. But in what way, where and when? I’d like to figure that question out. Mr. Weber, your idea is a good one. But I believe there is a better option. Mr. Gustafson, is it possible that we could call in our ships to bombard the fort?”                                                                                       “I’m afraid not, Maxim. Their range is not sufficient to fire upon the enemy base. But where exactly are you going with your spy plan, Mr. Ivanov?”                                                                                                                                                          “I think we should attempt to kidnap Blue Potato,” said Ivanov, causing multiple gasps in response. “We have the loyal and expertly-trained agents available to conduct such a maneuver, no?”                                                                                                                       

At the same time, deep inside Fort Toronto, Blue Potato conferred with his advisors in an underground bunker. “Gentlemen,” he began, “we are in a very constrained and challenging situation. We have only two generals here, not including myself, and we are cut off from the outside world. We have just a handful of troops to defend us. The enemy has thoroughly surrounded this fortress. Their warships control the Great Lakes, their troops march across the region. Our own forces will take months to travel here by land. Our cavalry was luckily able to capture the European artillery, and that is why our walls have not been bombarded to ashes. But they are bringing in replacement guns, and those will arrive soon. We must somehow drive away the enemy forces, and their ships, before the artillery shows up, or we are all going to die. Does anyone have a solution?” asked Blue Potato with a grim expression on his face.                                                                                                                                                                                                        “Ahem, Chief, but I believe the best way to handle this is by waging a battle. Let’s invite Gustafson onto the plains so our armies can duke it out. I see no better path to take. That is all,” finished Hiawatha.                                                                                          “And you have nothing, Mr. Blood Drawer, my right hand man?”                                                                                                                                                              “I am afraid not, Chief. I completely agree with Pollum. We have three options: surrender now, sit tight, keep the enemy away for as long as we can while waiting for rescue, or take the fight to them.”                                                                                                                 “I see. As Chief of the Dakota Confederacy, I hereby declare that we are going to do battle against the European empire!” Everyone in the room stood up from their seats, about to applaud. “Wait! Wait! I’m not done. Sit back down.” Everyone sat down. “I declare total war on Europe, their allies and associates! I declare that the one to deliver this offer of battle to General Gustafson shall be…Hollow Water, ambassador to Europe.”                                             “Hold on, Chief. Is he even available? I’ve never seen him in the fort before,” asked Blood Drawer.                                                            “Yes, he is,” replied Blue Potato. The door opened, and Hollow Water stepped into the room.                                            “Here I am,” he said. “I accept your mission, Chief. As for my limited appearances, I’ve spent most of my time reading famous works of literature and novels, looking at quotations, and strengthening my vocabulary. By doing all that in my quarters, I’ve left the home very little. So it makes sense why you’ve rarely seen me.”                                                                    “I’m glad to hear to that you accept, Hollow Water. Now go out there and make us proud by inviting Gustafson to a battle. Please have him show up on the plains just north of here, near the deserted farmhouse, on May 5th. We will be waiting for him there. This shall be our last stand.”                                                                                         

In the early hours of April 28th, Hollow Water approached the walls of Fort Ottawa, closely followed by 50 bodyguards, including Hiawatha. Telling his guards to stay behind, he rode up alone to the gate, accompanied only by an interpreter, carrying a white flag so that the Europeans would not fire at him. One of the sentries on the wall yelled down: “Who goes there?”                                                                                                                                                                       “It is I, Hollow Water,” responded the interpreter. “I am his translator. We are here to speak with General Elias Gustafson. Blue Potato has a message for him.”                                                                                                                                          The two sentries on the wall held a whispered conversation for a minute, then the first one who had spoken dropped down out of sight. Fifteen minutes later, a side door opened. Two large, strongly-built men who Hollow Water recognized as police officers walked out and began patting down and searching the two natives. After two minutes of sifting through their clothes and hair, the officers stood aside and allowed General Gustafson to pass. Three servants placed a wooden table and three stools down between the three men. Behind the general stood the two police officers and 3 tall, muscular Russian Imperial guards. Gustafson asked the interpreter if he and the ambassador would like a drink. “He would like to know if you want a beverage, sir,” said the translator.                      “No, no, I’m good,” replied Hollow Water, making a “no” sign with his hands. Gustafson smiled, and then turned to the interpreter. “How about you, my friend? Do you desire anything to drink?”                                                                                                              “I’ll have a cup of coffee, thank you,” said the interpreter. Gustafson called a servant over and requested a glass of orange juice and coffee, and then returned to the conversation. “Now then,” started Hollow Water, “I am here to deliver this message to you.” He pulled out a scroll from his pocket. “Blue Potato kindly invites you to do battle with his army on the plains just southwest of here, about 2 miles northeast of Fort Toronto, with the farmhouse. I give my solemn word to you that I will not conduct any mischief either. Now, I must ask, when should I bring my forces to fight you?”                                                                                                                                                                                                   “Blue Potato insists that it be on May 5th, and initiate around 9AM. Is that all right with you?”                                                          “Yes, we will be ready and prepared.” The server arrived with the drinks. “Mr. Diplomat, are we done here?”                               “Indeed we are. I will deliver your response to Blue Potato promptly.” Hollow Water placed the scroll back into his pocket. “But we wouldn’t want to waste these drinks, would we? I’d be happy to have a brief, friendly chat with you, Elias. I’ve already met your predecessors and associates Bellefleur and Gummen multiple times in the past, but I’ve never been properly acquainted with you.” The interpreter translated the message.                                                                                           “Alright then. I suppose I can wait a few minutes since we met so early this morning. Go ahead. Ask me some questions.”                           

After a lovely discussion with the general, Hollow Water returned back to base at a slow gallop in a very jolly mood. Hiawatha was perplexed at his attitude, but refrained from commenting. Upon entering the fortress, he went straight to the underground bunker and headed towards Blue Potato’s room. He was sitting at his desk, deep in a fictional novel as the ambassador opened the door. “Excuse me, sir, but I have Gustafson’s response.”                                                                    “Oh, goodness me yes! Please tell me now, Hollow Water. What in heavens did he say?” Blue Potato demanded.                     “He accepted your offer, and claims that he’ll be ready with his army on the plains at 9AM on May 5th.”                                  “Perfect. Now we’ve gotten him to swear to be honest, but we don’t need to follow our obligations. I’ve timed this very precisely. My agents in Québec sabotaged their artillery. It won’t be arriving at Fort Ottawa until May 8th. Therefore, they’ll have no guns while we possess their best. At the same time, we can fortify the farmhouse and Gustafson will have no choice but to assault it. I’m about to leave for the conference room to discuss strategy with Blood Drawer and the others. You coming?”                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   “No thanks, Chief. You have a brilliant plan and all, but I’d like to continue my reading like you just were. Dawn is just right for focus and concentration, allowing you to truly embrace the text.”                                                                                                       “Suit yourself.” With that, the chief walked past Hollow Water and out the door.

Gustafson, meanwhile, was holding his own council of war on April 30th, the day before his army began marching towards the battlefield. This time, however, it was held aboard the docked 105-gun Deutschland. “Gentlemen,” addressed General Gustafson. “We are about to fight the final battle. Should we achieve victory, it may end the war. Should we suffer defeat, we will be utterly humiliated and forced to retreat. Our containment of Fort Toronto will have to be concluded. We simply cannot fail this one time. It is very possibly the only opportunity we will ever get of its kind. Now, my fellow generals, let’s discuss how we are going to approach this battle. First of all, Maxim, I would like to appoint you my commander of the 2nd, 4th and 5th divisions, in addition to the Russian Imperial Guard. Secondly, I expect the natives are going to seize control of this farmhouse early on. How can we attain control without losing thousands of irreplaceable men and supplies—”                                                                                                                                                                       “Sir! Sir!” cried a messenger, running into the conference room.                                                                                                                 “What is it?” cried Gustafson. “I’m in the middle of a very important meeting!”                                                                                               “We just discovered that there were double agents trying to sabotage our artillery! Four men dressed in dark clothing placed several barrels of gunpowder near the replacement cannons while the horses towing them sat down for a rest. However, a farmer who happened to riding his mule to the city that day spotted the men fleeing, and alerted the artillerymen. With the help of the farmer, they were able to save most of the guns. Currently, the police and a few thousand of the major city garrisons are out scouring the countryside for the would-be-saboteurs. Would you mind lending a couple hundred of your Berlin officers for this manhunt?”                                                                                                                                                                                                       “I’d be delighted to have those fellows captured. Take 300 of my Berliners. I don’t mind.”                                                                   
“Good to hear, sir. I will leave you to your meeting now. It’s obviously the most important one so far.”                                “Commissioner Fusch, kindly loan the hunters 300 of your officers. Now, gentlemen, we are resuming our conference. How can we take the farmhouse without suffering massive casualties?                                                                                  “Now that we’ve got the cannonry, why don’t just bombard the farmhouse to bits?” suggested Weber.                                                            “Yes, in fact, that’s a great idea. Let’s do just that.”                                                                                                                           “So we’ll destroy the farmhouse with our artillery…now what?” asked General Ivanov.                                                                                              “Well, our spies report that the terrain is a relatively flat plain but our side does have a few miniature hills.”

Gustafson walked over to the front of the room and pulled down a scroll. “As one can see on this map of the battlefield, our side slopes down with a cliff in the middle, where the headquarters will be based. On both sides of the field are densely packed forests. The natives have created two large, hard to penetrate redoubts on both sides of the farmhouse. It will not be easy to assault them. Our cannons shall be placed all across our line. Hopefully, the angle of the slopes are high enough so that when the guns fire they do not hit our own men. Our cavalry will be positioned on the ends of the front. We can assume where the natives will place their men and robbed cannons by intelligence reports and data analysis, but it is uncertain where their cavalry shall be. Does anyone have any ideas as to how we should assault the redoubts? We are not going to just throw our men against them over and over and hope our numbers prevail. There are two large ponds in the middle of the field which might prove to be a hindrance.”               “What is the range of their and our artillery, General?” asked Weber.                                                                                                       “Theirs, being higher quality, can reach probably up to the edge of the pond nearest them. It is estimated that ours can only fire up to 50 feet before the edges of the pond nearest us. Does anyone know how any ideas? We can only bombard the farmhouse it is within range of our guns.”                                                                                                                                    “We could just wait it out. We do dominate the region at the moment. It’s impossible for the enemy to escape. We maintain control over every bit of land and sea in the sorrounding 90 miles at least. Why don’t we just meet them there and then stay idle. Let them make the first move. We have every reason to wait. On the other hand, the natives need to reach a conclusion quickly or they’ll be forced to either surrender or die. They’ve only got two seasons left before winter, and then when the cold and starvation kicks in…they’ll be regrettting not facing us earlier.”                               “No one’s got a better idea? Anyone?” No one spoke. “In that case, we are proceeding with Weber’s plan. In the meantime, I will formulate my own backup strategy in case the enemy has prepared a trick or two up his sleeve.”                  

May 5th, 1802. Blue Potato was in his headquarters on the cliff. Dawn was just breaking over the horizon. He exited his tent and took out his spyglass. The Europeans had not arrived yet. His native army was already setting up defenses. Atop the redoubts, artillerymen prepared the cannons and set down ammunition. On the fields below, troops put down stakes to repel cavalry. The native horsemen themselves were in hiding within the thick woodland on both sides, taking the utmost care to remain silent. At the same time, other men were getting in position inside the farmhouse. Blood Drawer had handpicked the strongest and most dedicated troops to guard it. Men removed the glass on the windows so that their muskets could fire through them and a several torches were brought in, along with a pot of oil, so that when the Europeans reached the entrance the boiling hot liquid could be poured down upon them. To conserve the limited supply of firearms and ammunition, only the troops at the window were equipped with muskets. Everyone else wielded either a sword, dagger, or axe. General Hiawatha was placed in control of the center, including the farmhouse. Blood Drawer was in charge of the two flanks and Blue Potato in overall command. He had sent a messenger to Bone Wrangler and Shark Bull for help at the beginning of the siege, in late March. Over a month later, there had been no response. Since the messenger had been sent on the most dangerous route, by ship across Lake Erie, there was ever reason to suspect that he had been killed by the European gunners. Still, Blue Potato secretly thought that, just maybe, one of the two commanders had been able to break the blockade and was on his way with an army.

Meanwhile, in Europe, the situation was not looking good. The Emperor and his commanders were away in Europe, leaving Prime Minister Joppel Toth as head of state. The crowd of protestors in Berlin had grown from about 40 to nearly 400. Unfortunately, since a few thousand Berlin police officers had been sent to North America with the emperor, the couple hundred remaining were not sufficient to keep back the crowd. The protestors, angry at how the government, especially Emperor Phillips, had not fulfilled its promises, began vandilizing homes and businesses across the city. 20 people in the mob, on May 2nd, manuevered to an area where there were no officers and made a run for city hall. Only one guard stood between them and the entrance. The civilians easily cut him down with their fists and stormed in. It took just a minute for them to locate the mayor’s office. Mayor Arnold Emmerich was working at his desk when the crowd broke inside. He grabbed a pistol kept inside his desk for emergencies and fired. One man was felled but the rest continued towards him. Emmerich was cornered, but fought, using the butt of his gun as a club, to the very end.

The following day, Sokolov, the justice minister, called out the German militia to restore order. Since Berlin had proved especially rowdy, he stationed the most troops there, 1,200 in total, compensating for the loss of the policemen. Sokolov had also received reports that a large lot of protestors in Paris had been inspired by the Berliners and were gathering in front of the military headquarters every morning. Luckily, the chances of an assault succeeding were unlikely, as the complex was guarded by 2,500 troops at all times, but nevertheless the justice minister gave the police strict orders to execute by the end of the month: “Rid the city of these unruly ruffians by words, or force if you must.” On May 11th, the mob of nearly 8,000 was confronted by a combined force of police and militia numbering close to 2,000. The comissioner bellowed out the order: “Leave now or we will open fire! I repeat, leave now or we will open fire!” But the crowd would not litsen. Ten minutes later, they were still shouting and chanting their discontent. Finally, the comissioner declared that it was time. The first line of officers raised their muskets and fired. Twenty civilians were killed instantly and many more wounded. There were screams heard and the crowd began to scatter. The first line of police kneeled to reload while the second fired. Just two minutes later, the streets were clear. All that remained were the dead bodies and blood.

7AM. The first European troops begin to arrive at the battlefield. General Elias Gustafson is their commander. He plants the flag of Europe at the very tip of the cliff. Then he heads back several hundred feet to the headquarters. “Mr. Ivanov, please oversee the preparation of our defenses and artillery setup while I converse with Mr. Weber here.”                                                                                                                                                                                                                   “Yes sir.”                                                                                                                                                                                        The European forces labored for the next hour on planting their own stakes, assembling the artillery, handing out arms and ammunition, setting up the headquarters and tents and flattening the ground. The cooks and servants, of which there were several hundred, began to prepare the 2nd breakfast of the morning (the first had been served around five). At 8:02, the head chef blew a trumpet, and all the soldiers lined up to receive their meal: sausages, an omelet, a scoop of fried potatoes, and optional corn bread all on a plate and served with a side of cold water or coffee for officers and above. Gustafson was not hungry due to his anxiousness at the coming battle and took only 3 bites of an apple before washing it down with a sip of wine. Breakfast had wrapped up by 8:55, and the battle opened a few minutes later with Blue Potato lighting the first cannon shot. The cannonball soared over the field and smashed into the hardended, dry dirt of the field. Although the natives expected some sort of a response, Gustafson ordered his men to do nothing but wait. Blue Potato, suspcious, simply sat down on a stool and did the same. But twenty minutes later, he realized what was really happening and told Blood Drawer to lead 2 light cavalrydivisions in an initial charge against the European left flank. The chief hoped to have the cavalry soften that flank, and then follow it up with waves of infantry and heavy cavalry assualts.

Gustafson, who had been writing out a letter to his mother in his tent, was startled was Weber opened the flap and cried: “Elias, they’re moving!” The general ran out of the tent and looked up. The enemy cavalry was indeed racing towards them. Gustafson  immediately shouted for Ivanov. “Maxim! Get those infantry in square! Now!” He then turned to the artillerymen. “Have those guns loaded and firing this second! The enemy is approaching!” Troops all over the front scrambled to get into position and the gunners frantically got their cannons ready. Boom! Boom! Boom! Several cannonballs were launched against the cavalry with devestating effect. The natives continued to race towards them, however. They were hit hard by musket fire coming from the infantry squares. The cavalry charged straight into the squares and began engaging in close combat with the squares. Following not far behind were 9,000 native infantry. About half carried muskets, most of them abandoned by or stolen from the Europeans, but a few sent from Africa. The rest were all equipped with heavy battle axes, spears or swords. On the infantry’s flank were 2,000 heavy cavalry, composed of stronger men wielding grander weapon and larger horses than the light. Astoundingly, none of Blue Potato’s troops present, not even the heavy cavalry considered his best soldiers on the field, wore body armor.

Just as the chief had hoped, by 10PM the light cavalry divisions had drawn the Europeans’ attention to their left flank. It therefore came as a surprise when the native warriors focused their attack on the right. The heavy cavalry managed to break through the first line of defenses and were about to reach the artillery emplacements, only to be deterred by a hasty last-minute charge of some 1,500 French lancers, directed by Gustafson himself. The European commander was in the thick of the fighting on the right flank, and later some of the artillerymen, being witnesses, would say that had it not been for his bold leadership the enemy horsmen would have breached the defenses. The heavy cavalry were closely trailed by 4 infantry battalions, which fought determinedly against the lancers atop the slope. After the heavy cavalry had been routed on the right flank, Gustafson decided to spare some losses among his lancers and ordered them to scatter. The native troops behind them were delighted, thinking they were fleeing, and sprinted forward only to be greeted by several rounds of grapeshot. It was the end for them, and the natives turned around, not to stop running until they reached the safety of their camp.

Blue Potato, watching everything through his spyglass while sitting on the cliff, had a frown on his face. He had lost a divison of heavy cavalry, two divisions of light cavalry and more than a few squadrons of infantry. The assault on the right flank which he had such high hopes for had been deterred by Gustafson’s abrupt arrival. His troops were fighting hard on the left flank, where the battle swung back and forth. Now, at 11AM, the Chief decided to send General Hiawatha with another 5,000 warriors against the left flank to keep up the pressure. However, the advancing troops caught Gustafson’s attention. Waiting for all the native forces to be packed into one mass fighting the European squares, Gustafson then launched 12,000 heavy cavalry under Weber’s command to sorround the horde. He was successful in trapping the native troops behind two large European divisions. It became a giant slaughter as Blue Potato’s men struggled to escape; most failed and were put to the sword. Within 15 minutes, thousands of freshly killed native bodies coated the field. Between 10-20 had managed to flee and survive. Blue Potato, who had watched the scene through his spyglass, was devestated. The battle was going horribly. He decided to stop the assaults and have everyone retire for lunch.

The native troops, already suffering from low morale due to a string of defeats during the morning, were not at all thrilled to discover that their “lunch” was, due to the European blockade, simply a slice of goat cheese with bread, a spoonful of roast beef, a handful of grapes and water from the fortress wells. On the other hand, the Europeans lunched on luxurious meals cooked by the army’s professional chefs. Choices for the troops, according to diaries and portions of official records, included:

·       Black rissoto and duck à l’orange with lemon gelato

·       Vegetable lasgana and Penne all'arrabbiata with iced tea

·       Roast beef, pea soup and Dutch apple pie

Everyone, except for the servants, were welcome to help themselves to beer and cold water, and due to a surprlus of food supplies from the transports coming from Europe every soldier was permitted a small second helping if desired. Gustafson himself, in a very good mood, ordered the first selection and after finishing his gelato slurped down two more bowls of pea soup. It was a hearty meal and afterwards most of the European troops had stuffed themselves silly, along with Gustafson himself. Ivanov was one of the only Europeans who had ate light and strolling through the camp at one on his horse shook his head in disbelief. Nearly all the soldiers were drunk or taking a nap on the grass. Half an hour later, the situation had not improved. Ivanov, taking matters into hand, began galloping through the European camp, blowing a horn while two of his subordinates followed on horseback, banging pots and pans together to make as much noise as possible. It was not long before everyone had awakened to find three massive native forces quickly advancing towards them.

“General! General!” Ivanov was in Gustafson’s tent, shaking him to try and get him awake. “Wake up! Wake up!”    Gustafson let out a long yawn. “What is it, Maxim? I’m trying to get a nap. That was one delicious lunch—”                            “Elias! The natives are attacking and everyone is asleep! We need our leader, now!” Just as Gustafson’s eyes widened with alarm, Ivanov forcefully pulled him out of the tent and into the sunlight. Blue Potato’s troops had indeed reached the bottom of the slope. A not fully awake Gustafson failed to take note that, strangely, only one line of native troops had come up this far. The remainder were waiting far behind, just out of reach of the artillery. He did, however, issue orders to his men. “Get those cannons pounding! Maxim! Bring the 2nd and 3rd heavy cavalry divisions to the front! Weber, lead the 5th, 8th and 9th line infantry divisions here immediately!” Just as he had expected, the first line of natives broke and ran, and the rest of their attacking forces similarly began retreating. Gustafson immediately ordered Ivanov to pursue with his cavalry, but he had made a mistake in sending heavy instead of light cavalry. Abruptly, 3 battalions of native horsemen charged out of the forest and engaged Ivanov’s divisions, preventing them from being able to pursue. The cavalry battle was to rage for hours on the edges of the tear-shaped pond as both sides sent reinforcements.

Meanwhile, Gustafson ordered at 1PM for Weber to lead 3 infantry divisions against the farmhouse in the deep center of the native line. It was guarded by artillery on both sides, and the twin redoubts. To keep the slingers, javelinists and musketmen atop the first redoubt busy, Gustafson sent in the elite Austrian jäger battalion commanded by captain Franz Müller. For the second one, he hesitantly ordered Commissioner Felix Fusch to assault it with his Berlin officers. It took a grueling 80 minutes for the European forces to reach the base of the redoubts, and after passing the larger of the two ponds (they purposely avoided the other for fear of being entangled in the cavalry fight) the native artillery opened up on them. As soon as they passed the pond, Weber’s men began running in order to avoid as much of the bombardment as they could. Luckily, Blue Potato had the finest guns but lacked the operators, and therefore the rate of fire was slow and inaccurate. Soon, after suffering not too many losses, Weber reached the center of the native line. While Fusch’s officers handled the right redoubt and the jägers the left, General Weber ran his infantry straight up to the farmhouse. As the troops neared, native musketmen in and outside the farmhouse fired several volleys at the Europeans, taking out many. But Weber’s men kept on coming, and the divisions crashed straight into the farmhouse door, which was both bolted and barricaded. Blood Drawer, who was in command of the farmhouse, ordered 900 warriors, carrying longswords and axes, to attack. Surprising the Europeans, they initially pushed them back several feet. Hand-to-hand combat ensued, leaving Weber at a serious disadvantage because the native musketmen continued to pour fire on them from the second story. But his men pressed on, and eventually they routed the warriors. His infantrymen charged into the farmhouse and engaged the troops inside in a very fierce battle.

3PM. The farmhouse and the tear-drop pond are both areas of extreme, prolonged combat. The cavalry battle carries on as Blue Potato and Gustafson both send more and more of their best horsemen into the fight. Weber struggles to force himself into the farmhouse, but for now the natives have been successful in keeping them out. Although the Europeans could use artillery against the cavalry and the natives the Europeans at the farmhouse, neither will for fear of hitting their own men. With both areas covered by his two best and highes-ranking commanders, Gustafson feels secure and turns away from the field. He calls a meeting in the dining hall (simply a large tent fit to accommodate 40-50 people) with Emperor Phillips and three of his subordinates, Edgar Grigorov, a Bulgarian, Samuel Leroy, from France, and Paul Saminov, the captain of the Russian Imperial Guard. A servant brought them coffee and Gustafson began. “Gentlemen, I fear that Ivanov was right. We cannot win this battle through brute force alone. He and Weber are busy keeping the natives’ defenders and cavalry occupied. Meanwhile, I think the time is ripe for an assassination. Unfortunately, the jägers are not available, but we do have a few agents ready and waiting, isn’t that right, Mr. Leroy?”                                                                                                                                                                                       “Oh yes, Elias. I have 10 men with me who am prepared to undertake such a mission. But may I ask who our target is, General?”                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     “Why, Blue Potato, of course. If he is taken out, the remainder of the enemy army will surely collapse with him.”             “How should we take him out, sir?” asked Leroy. “I’m unable to find an open route where we could just rush up and kill him.”                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                “Yes, the native headquarters is inconveniently located just behind the farmhouse,” replied Gustafson, who stood up and pulled out a large scroll from his coat pocket, which turned out to be a map. Pointing to it, he said: “We need to somehow get past all those defenders, which won’t be easy. They’ve got these huge redoubts on both sides and although going through the farmhouse is the most straightforward, it’s simply impenetrable. I’m not sure about going around either, since chances are they’ve got reserves behind the redoubts that we just can’t see, and I’d hate for our elite assassins to run into those. Your highness, have you any ideas?”                                                                                                          “As a matter of fact I do,” started the emperor. “Samuel, why don’t you and your boys dress up in native garb and pretend to be servants for the chief? That way you can get near him, hopefully in his tent. Then, when it’s safe, stick your knife through him and the job is done. Make a run for it and we’ll be waiting for you.”                                                                        “I’m not too sure about this, General,” said Leroy. “I’d rather just work on defeating their army. I don’t know if killing their Chief is the best way to go about this…”    

An hour later, Leroy and his group of agents were on horseback, riding through the thick forest, trying to get behind native lines. Just as they got off their saddles and approcahed the camp, a Dakota sentry stopped them. “Excuse me, but what are you doing?” he asked angrily. “Why were you in the forest? You look like servants to me. There’s no business for you to be conducting in there.”                                                                                                                                                                   Leroy winked to one of the agents, indicating that they were all to go along with the plan now, as the guard was fooled by their attire and face paint. He then began speaking in heavily accented Dakota language. “We was trying to find food for the Chief, but was unable locate any. We are going back to him to apologize. Do let us in please.”               “Hmm…what’s with the accents? I’ve never heard of any funny-sounding peoples like you before…where are you all from?”                                                                                                                                                                                            “Please,” said Leroy, “just allow us to enter. We must apologize to the Chief.”                                                                                                                                      “Fine, but that shouldn’t take too long, should it? I’m just gonna hold this one here hostage,” said the sentry as he snapped his fingers, “until you return. I’ll give you 10 minutes, how about that?” Another guard appeared and ushered one of the agents away with his spear. “If you don’t show up right here on time, this one gets it.”                                    With that, the guard left and as soon as he was out of earshot, Leroy turned to his agents. “Boys, we’ve got to find the Chief’s tent, quickly, or otherwise our pal there gets a knife in his ribs. Let’s go. Follow my lead.”                                Leroy began walking towards a tent several yards away. He assumed it was Blue Potato’s since it had two guards on the outside and a flag flying beside it. His men followed. As they had all expected, once Leroy got within two feet of the tent one of the guards stopped him. “Whoa there. What do you think you’re doing? Who are you?”                                    “We are servants. We come to apologize to Chief because we was unable to find food for Chief. Please let us in.”               “Gee, your speaking skills are rubbish. I can’t believe you were hired to work for us. Ah well, I guess I can let you in if my buddy here supervises. But I’ll let have to inspect you first. Come over here.”                                                “Why you got to inspect us?” asked Leroy.                                                                                                                                                                                           “So we can make sure you ain’t carrying no weapons of any sort.” The guard reached to Leroy and began to pat him down, but just as he was about to feel the dagger, Leroy pulled it out and in one lightning-fast movement, sliced off the guard’s hand. “AAAAIIIIIIIIIEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEEE!” he screamed in pain, jumping backwards and tripping over a stone. “Luca!” he cried. “Get them, now!” The other guard took out his sword and jumped Leroy, engaging them in a wrestling match. The other agents were stunned for a moment but then sprung into action. Two of them joined in to free Leroy from the guard while the rest entered the tent. They were all killed instantly by a whiff of grapeshot.

“I am most humbled at your improvisation, Shark Bull,” remarked Blue Potato.                                                                     “I’m flattered, Chief,” he replied, “but really, it wasn’t that difficult. We just appeared on the other side of the camp, discovered that you were being threatened, and so we tried to make as little noise as possible while approaching your tent and informing you of the situation. Luckily, the fight at the farmhouse and the assassins’ combat with your guards were loud enough to cover your escape and your replacement by a cannon. It was already loaded, so we just waited for them to open the flap. When they did, well, it was all over.”                                                                                             “I have to say, Shark Bull, for such an astonishing and impressive feat, saving my life, you may have very well earned yourself some rewards, maybe even a promotion. I owe you dearly, General. But first—”                                                                          “Yes. Don’t worry, Chief, I’ll have this battle wrapped up in no time.” Immediately, he jumped on his horse and galloped off to lead his army.                                                                                                                                                                                   “Men,” Shark Bull cried. “We are now to send those Europeans packing. Musketmen, begin firing at the enemy troops sorrounding the farmhouse. Cavalry, ambush those jägers on the right. Warriors, fight off the policemen on the left. Let’s move, move move!”                                                                                                                                                                “Yes sir!”

Gustafson was on his way to the front, ready to rally his troops on, when he saw what was happening. It was obvious that the assassionation attempt had failed. But now what was going on? The Berlin officers, who he had reinforced with two battalions of line infantry, were falling back from the redoubts, struggling to fight off a new group of hardened native warriors. The jägers were all of a sudden charged by several hundred native light cavalry. How foolish he had been for not ordering Müller to plant stakes on all sides! Gustafson cursed quietly, but there was nothing that could be done now. Both flanks were now routing. It was too late to have the center retreat…hopefully Weber and at least a reasonable portion of his men managed to flee safely.

Gustafson quickly raced back to his headquarters and gave the most feared order for a general retreat along the entire front. However, Ivanov persuaded him to try one last time for a victory. The Russian Imperial Guard was sent in, along with 8,000 more infantry whose formations still remained completely intact. The Guards started off at a walk towards the native lines, and managed to advance 35 yards beyond the ponds, 45 yards, 55 yards, nearly reaching the redoubts! The Imperial Guard, being the most elite and respected of all the European troops on the field that day, continued their walk despite losing many to the ferocious native artillery fire. But when they came within range of the native muskets, everything fell apart. The Russians suffered over 200 casualties in just a few seconds at first. They were able to successfully fire a few volleys of their own in response, and felled many natives lined up below the redoubts. But soon, they were having their ranks filled by common line infantry, and 20 minutes later, it all fell apart. Paul Saminov, the Guard’s captain, stayed behind with his men and fought to the very end, allowing the rest of the army of make a hasty escape. Although the Guard’s accompanying infantry fled before long, it is said that not a single one of the Russian Imperial Guard lived after that day. 10 years after the battle, Gustafson would declare while honoring their offical memorial in Moscow that the unit was not to ever be revived, because no soldiers, no matter how qualified, could ever replace those who fatefully died that day.

Blue Potato and his men were beyond ecstatic. He decided to first take care of the bodies that night and clean up the battlefield, then begin a week’s worth of celebrations the following afternoon at 5PM. The next day, on May 6th, proved very busy for the head chef, Buffalo Rider, who oversaw a massive cooking session as over 900 cooks prepared thousands of dishes for the entire army present at Fort Toronto that day. This was made much easier by the fact that the Europeans had begun pulling out their blockade, and in the process had stated that they would not attack any native ships traveling through the Great Lakes. Blue Potato, exhuasted from the previous day, had ruled to stay in the underground bunker until the start of the celebration. He slept through the entire morning, ate lunch, then wrote in his diary and read one of his favorite books until 4:30. Stepping out of his quarters, he was astonished to see an incredibly busy scene. Buffalo Rider was supervising the prepartion of a gigantic pot of stew. Multiple jumbo bowls of guacamole, baked beans, smoked salmon, and fried potatoes with onions were brought out as appetizers until dinner was served at 7. It was to be a grand week of celebrations. The Chief was scheduled to open and conclude the celebrations, and deliver an opening address, also the victory speech, starting at 6:40 and lasting until dinnertime. For the following 6 days, there would be games, a ceremony for those who died, anti-European rallies and events, mass entertainment in the Dakota capital of Eropet, a mock reconstruction of the battle on the same site, and plays held in the courtyard of Fort Toronto, many of which were classics by European and native playwrights (of course, any offenses to natives were ommitted).

Meanwhile, Gustafson had retreated with the remains of his army back to Fort Ottawa. From there, he boarded his flagship, the Northumberland. He then ordered the ship to sail back to Europe. His top advisors protested, saying it would be very bad for his public image if he just took off without warning, and leaving the emperor behind, but the general, depressed and with a blurred mind, did not listen. Amazingly, he did even bother to order escorts, and simply left into the Atlantic, with only 150 or so guards/gunners aboard. The ship was not even at full strength, but Gustafson did so anyway. Emperor Phillips, hearing of his abrupt departure a few days later, was perplexed and nonplussed. He eventually decided that he would follow him, and discuss the failure with him and the general’s staff. Although he did not share his thoughts with anyone, and only jotted down brief passages in his diary (which he kept sealed up in a secret location), the emperor privately believed that Gustafson was not solely responsible for the defeat, nor did even the majority of the blame fall on him. He thought the most important aspect of the fight that day had been conducted in an area far from the battlefield itself.

In Africa, King Ubinu II was not feeling well. A European agent had planted a bomb inside the Mendi, a 70-gun warship on which he had traveling in the Red Sea en route to South Africa. Most of the passengers aboard had survived with only minor injuries, but the king had been launched off the ship and landed on the deck of an escort vessel. He had suffered a fractured leg, several snapped ribs and a chipped tooth. Royal guards had the king sailed to a nearby inn, and 20 messengers were sent out to search for a doctor. A local farmer informed them that a very qualified doctor was available in Djbouti, only 15 miles away, but by then Ubinu had already lost a significant amount of blood.

Blue Potato was just wrapping up his victory celebrations on May 13th, 1802. It had been a marvelous week in Eropet. Now he and his advisors were busy planning a summer campaign that would capture Ottawa and Montréal, then maintain that position for 8 months. In the spring of 1803 they would launch several simultaneous attacks, confuse the Europeans and win Québec. Soon the war would all be over. However, Blood Drawer had decided that for it to have the greatest chance of success, the Africans’ help would be required. Unfortunately, at the moment, the chances of the Africans providing support were not promising.

In conference room A of his HQ in Paris, Emperor Phillips led the review of the unsuccessful 1802 spring campaign. “Gustafson, your plan was brilliant. I do not blame you for the failure.”               

“But sir!” pleaded Weber. “He was the commander! He was the one who sent our troops to their deaths! Now we’ve lost the Russian Imperial Guard, the Austrian jägers and those Berlin police officers for nothing—”                               “Hold it, Alexander. That may have been due to him, but let me restate myself. I do not fully blame him. I say Admiral Vincent here is far more at fault here.”                                                                                                     “What! Why would you point fingers at me!” cried the admiral. “If anything, you should be firing him!”                      “Gustafson did what he could. I’m afraid it’s your navy that let victory slip out of our hands. You allowed a pathetic, worthless, motley fleet of native ships get past your blockade. Don’t mock what you can’t handle, Herbert.”                         “But, but, you don’t understand, your highness. Those were some of the most poorly-trained, least experienced sailors in the world! Going up against the finest in Europe!”                                                                                                         “All the more reason why it shouldn’t have happened. You permitting Shark Bull and his army to enter the battlefield turned the tables on us. Gustafson wasn’t prepared for that. Your mission was simple. Keep those ****ing natives out. It was a very important one, proven by the result of this latest battle.”                                              “I, I, I…Emperor, this is madness. Why don’t you fire the men on those ships? Why don’t you execute the captain of that ship instead?”                                                                                                                                                           “As a matter of fact, Herbert, I already have. Sadly, because you were the supervising admiral, you’re going down with them. I’m afraid I’m going to have to ask you to head to your office and clear out your room. We are going to have some new brains heading the best navy in the world.”                                                                                               “Yes, your highness.” Admiral Vincent slowly walked out of the room with a grim look on his face.                                          “Now, gentlemen, Canada is in peril. We’ve lost an army, but we haven’t lost the war. I’m sure we can turn this around.”                                                                                                                                                                                         “Emperor Phillips, perhaps you haven’t heard,” began Justice Minister Sokolov. “But we are experiencing problems in some of our major cities. The populace is upset. We need to gain a victory, and fast.”                                                                                      “I was thinking,” said Weber, “perhaps we follow through with Collingwood’s earlier, abandoned plan.”                                       “Are you saying that we should send 4 armies into Mexico?” asked a stunned Phillips.                                                                              “That’s exactly what I’m saying.”

 

King Ubinu was on his deathbed. He had arrived at the doctor’s office several hours too late. “Please, Alaq, you cannot die,” begged a sobbing General Okoro, who kneeled at his bedside.                                                                            “One cannot live forever,” replied Ubinu in a weak voice. “I met my end at the hands of an evil assasin.”                            “But who will succeed you, sir? You have no children.”                                    

“Why, you will, Ahmed. I have faith in you. You are my closest advisor and general of the armies. I hereby give all my earthly powers and command to you. As my last wish, I ask that you take revenge on those Europeans. They must be responsible for my death. Slay them all and I will be satisified.” The king’s eyelids began to fall.                                         “No, no, Alaq! You cannot die! Not now! No! No! Nnnnooooooooooo!!!” Okoro grabbed the king and shook him several times, but there was no response.                                                                                                                                                                              “Okoro, I bid you farewell.” And with that, the king descended into the afterlife with the general at his side, bawling. 

July 1st, 1802. Generals Weber and Ivanov were once again sitting in conference room A in Paris headquarters. Except this time, the emperor was not present, just the general’s staff. Phillips, in desperate of some new, bold strategy to change public opinion of the war, had swiftly approved Weber’s plan to invade Mexico. “We’re lucky he accepted, aren’t we, Alexander?” remarked Ivanov.                                                                                                                                                      “Well, I’m sure nearly getting killed in that riot a fortnight past changed his mind.”                                                                                           “Anyways,” started Ivanov, “some very important news came from the Africans. Their new king, Okoro, has betrayed his allies the natives and asked if we could use their assistance.”                                                                                                                        “Seriously, Maxim?” responded Weber.                                                                                                                                  “Yes. His second-in-command, Juke Bomani, arrived a few days ago, and told us that Okoro was willing to negotiate a deal. He said we’re a lot more powerful and worth making an alliance with than those native savages.”               “Wow,” replied Weber. “Mr. Weis, what do you think we should do?”                                                                                                “Um…why are you asking me, Mr. Weber? You make the executive decision, right?”                                                                                         “Ha ha,” laughed Weber. “That’s correct. I say we should accept the deal.” He then leaned over and whispered in Ivanov’s ear: “After all, we can always follow in their footsteps and double-cross them as well. They can help us in our invasion of Mexico. Once we have North America under our heel, we can turn southward and conquer Africa as well. I would throw in Asia too, but they’re too powerful and we can’t afford to keep the war going that long.”                            “Sounds fantastic. Alexander and me have agreed to negotiate. We will offer them Gibraltar and £90,000 worth in gold, in exchange for support in defeating the natives and Egypt. Does anyone have any objections?”                                       “If not, then we will seek approval from the emperor right now. This meeting is hereby dismissed,” stated Weber.

“Blood Drawer, are your men ready?” asked Blue Potato. It was June 2nd, and they were lying behind some bushes on the edge of Fort Ottawa.                                                                                                                                                                 “Of course, Chief. All 40,000 of them. We have already worked up the plan for this summer. We shall—”                                          “Not now, Blood Drawer. Tell me more once you have successfully taken this fortress.”                                                                                          “Yes, Chief. Shark Bull!” he yelled. Suddenly, 25 cannons fired at once, bombarding the walls of Fort Ottawa and severely damaging them. A portion of the front began to collapse, taking down three patrolling sentries with it.                “Now!” screamed Blue Potato. His generals Shark Bull and Blood Drawer abruptly charged in with their heavy infantry, who were all mounted on horseback, enabling them to move quicker. We greatly appreciated receiving your last letter. You know that we have only the utmost respect for you. Everyone at home is cheering for you. The neighbors praise you. Your aunts and uncles are rooting for you. Your cousins wake up every day thinking of you. We are all eagerly awaiting news of your imminent victory.

Elena went off to university last month. We trust she will do well on her own. There are many wonders and sights in Zurich. She sent us a wonderful postcard a week ago. She says she likes all of her classes, and there are many places to explore in her free time. She is thinking of finding a boyfriend soon.

The life is good back home. We went to the farmer’s yesterday. There were a great many foods on sale imported from North America: we met a peddler who served in the war and brought back with him basketfuls of tomatoes and potatoes. Being the parents of a two soldiers, he offered us a very fair price! We bought a pound of them and made us a great batch of soup that will last the entire week.

The church is filled with fathers, mothers, and daughters every Sunday. Reverend Queller begins and ends each of his sermons by reading the following passage from the holy scriptures: ‘Solomon led his people through the towns and villages. They saw destruction and death before them. They left rejuvenation and revial after them. When they finally reached the Western province, he provided an offering atop the highest altar. And he continued to place an offering every fortnight, until one day he himself sat on the altar and offered himself to God.’ He reminds us that you and everyone around you has made the ultimate sacrifice, one for which we shall be forever in your debt. All the little boys in town grow up hoping to be like you someday.

We are all hoping fervently for your continued success. Our thoughts and prayers go out to you.

Always yours,

Marie and Augustus”

You read the rest of the letters. Stevenson makes a few changes here and there, but few edits are deemed necessary. The letters are turned over to Marcian, who reads them in his loud, resonating voice as you and the rest of your regiment sit by your respective campfires.The soldiers are tired, but they react positively to the letters. After assigning a few corporals to join you on sentry duty, the captain retires to his tent at midnight, as does the remainder of the Third Swiss Army. Spyglass in hand, you climb into the hastily built outpost on the edge of the camp, where you prepare to spend the next six hours.

Shortly after 1am, you hear a knock at the door. A young lady, a couple inches shorter than you with long brown hair, steps forward to greet you.

“Pardon me, officer. I’m Phoebe Dunbury, sister to Sergeant Dunbury. I came to see how you were doing.”

Mundane as sentry duty usually is, this visit comes as a pleasant surprise. “Miss Dunbury, it’s so nice of you to drop by. I am Sergeant Polagard.” you say. “I didn’t know Sergeant Dunbury had any siblings.”

“He doesn’t talk about me very much. Our parents divorced when I was very young: we were raised in separate households. He brought me with him only because our father insisted. Yet I yearn for the open pastures back in Belgium. Do you mind if I join you?”

“Not at all,” you respond, offering her a seat. There is not much resemblance, you think. She must be much younger than the sergeant.

“Oh, how dull the camp can be at this hour. You must be bored out of your mind.”

“Indeed. To what do I owe the pleasure of your company?” you ask.

“Would you believe it: there are only two women in this regiment. Myself, and seventy-year-old Miss Carter, the mother of Lieutenant Carter. She is a kind old lady, but after listening to her tell the same story for the umpteenth time … I’m not sure whether to laugh or cry!”

“It’s a shame,” you say, “that Miss Carter doesn’t know how to appreciate someone like you.”

“Oh, if only. There are so few men worth befriending. All they ever do is smoke cigarettes and play their confounded gambling games.”

“Why don’t you tell me a little more about yourself, Miss Dunbury?” you say.

“You are the first one to ask me that since I got here. You’re alright, Sergeant Polagard!” She puts his hand on top of yours.

The shift flys by as you engage in a lengthy conversation with Miss Dunbury that continues until sunrise. Listening to her describe her childhood, you can’t help but be drawn to her soft blue eyes, her sweet melodious voice, her smooth soft skin … by the time you part ways for breakfast, you want nothing more than to be locked in an eternal, tender embrace with Phoebe.

 

 Your regiment advances rapidly toward Montreal. Aside from reconnassiance, you spend most of your time reading, eating, and engaging in small talk with the other sergeants. A few days later, Captain Stevenson approaches you in the midst of a thunderstorm.

“Sergeant Polagard. Would you come with me, please? Colonel Stanley wishes to see you,” he say.

“Certainly.” Your answer is calm and professional, though you can’t help but wonder what the leader of your regiment could possibly want with you. He is a private man, reserved in personality as well as demeanor, and you rarely ever see him.

Rain pouring onto your hat, you walk with the captain in silence until you arrive at a large grey tent at the edge of the camp. A thin, elderly man with greying hair is inside with his back to you. He wears a dark suit with a blue cravat. At the sound of your entrance, he turns around.

“Avraham! Welcome. And you must be —”

“Colonel, this is Sergeant Polagard,” the captain says.

“Ah, yes. I believe we’ve met. Now, let us dipense with the pleasantries,” the colonel says before you have a chance to speak. “Do you know why I’ve called in today?”

“No, sir,” you reply.

“You are here because, if I am not mistaken, it seems you entertained an uninvited guest during sentry duty on Sunday. Do not bother denying it. Captain Stevenson can corrobate the claim.”

“Sir, I apologize for my mistake. Miss Dunbury was merely looking for company. What was I to do, slam the door in her face?”

“Yes, if need be! Social intercourse is yours to be had during on your personal time, but every minute you spend chitchatting on sentry duty compromises our entire mission!” Colonel Stanley says with visible agitation, pounding his desk. “Were Miss Dunbury not related to one of our officers, she would be expelled immediately. Fortunately for you, I will allow her to stay, provided you limit your interactions with her. As our handbook clearly states, promiscuity and matrimony are forbidden in our ranks. I will let you off with a warning. Should I catch you out of place again, you will be sent straight home with no consolation.”

“Understood,” you say, shaken at the colonel’s stern tone.

“No word of this shall reach the men, captain.”

“I will make sure of it,” says Captain Stevenson.

“Any questions?”

“No, sir.”

“Then that will be all. Dismissed.”

You leave the tent as the captain stays behind to talk with the colonel, waiting until you are out of earshot to resume their conversation. With the rain still falling, you return to your own tent and resolve to whittle away the remainder of the day in solitude.

Yet it is impossible to forget about Phoebe. You quickly write out a note to her explaining the circumstances, then place it into an envelope.

“You there!” you say to a messenger boy outside. “Please have this delivered to Miss Phoebe Dunbury.”

“Yes, sergeant Polagard.”

 

A week passes by before your regiment arrives at the designated rendezvous point. Riding at the head of the column at daybreak, you hear the hooves of Captain Stevenson’s horse approach.

“Staff sergeant Ricardo tells me these are the coordinates Colonel Pendergast sent us. But where is he?”

“I don’t see anything,” you say, pulling out your spyglass. Then, out of the corner of your eye, you spot a European flag amidst the snow.

“There!” you exclaim, extending a black-gloved finger in the direction of the flag.

You tell your men to stay behind while you ride with Stevenson and Ricardo to investigate. The flag is planted firmly in the ground, but there doesn’t seem to be anything around it. All you see are miles and miles of snow, at least a foot thick. Something is wrong.

“Staff sergeant Ricardo! Where is the Fourth French Army?” the captain yells.

“I don’t know, sir. According to my compass, these are the exact coordinates. Pendergast’s army should have arrived days ago.”

As you dismount from your horse and walk closer to the flag, your jackboot catches on something buried in the snow, almost causing you to fall over. Intrigued, you reach down and dig with your hands. What you find shocks you beyond belief.

It is Colonel Pendergast, his dead face preserved fully intact by the thick blanket of snow. You let out a muted scream. Stevenson and Ricardo rush over, and then the three of you uncover the rest of the body. Seeing that his dress uniform has been penetrated with a stab wound, you reckon that whatever killed him — and the snowfall, by extension— was a recent occurrence.

“What happened here?” Ricardo asks.

“We will answer that soon enough,” the captain says. “Alert Colonel Stanley immediately.”

“Yes, sir.”

The rest of the regiment is soon on the scene, and it gradually becomes clear that Pendergast’s death was not an isolated event: caught by surprise, the Fourth French Army was ambushed at the rendezvous point and slaughtered to a man. When Colonel Stanley arrives, he is speechless at first. After consulting with the captain, he gives the order to continue marching south as planned. “It is the only honorable way to acknolwedge the fallen,” he says.

Pendergast and a few others are dug graves, but there is simply not enough time. The snow will have to suffice.

As Gustafson’s intelligence predicted, the natives have failed to deploy any troops to the area. The enemy towns and villages in your path are well stocked and completely undefended. You and your fellow sergeants see to it that the women and children are protected, but there are few other restrictions. Hungry for retribution, your men loot and ransack the houses before burning them to the ground. A warehouse filled to the brim with barrels of salted bear meat and mackerel is emptied and then put to the torch. A small pond in the center of the town is filled with dirt and sod. Having set the rest ablaze, you eventually come to the largest building: the meeting hall. Unwilling to waste excessive amounts of wood, you and your men spend the night inside, light half a dozen campfires, and simply do not put them out when you depart.

As your regiment begins marching north, a rider atop a black stallion appears on the horizon. You bring the column to a halt. As he approaches, you realize it is one of Field Marshal Gustafson’s messengers. Panting breathlessly, he places a scroll in your hand.

“Message from Field Marshal Gustafson to Colonel Stanley.”

“Did you come alone?” you ask.

“No sir. Two others were assigned to accompany me, but they went missing after a snowstorm. I believe they have perished in the cold.”

“And where you are headed?” you say, wondering if this is the same snowstorm that buried Pendergast’s army.

“Back to Quebec, sir. Same direction as you are.”

“Then by all means, join us.” The messenger boy, clearly grateful, shakes yor hand warmly and offers several words of gratitude. You nod politely, turning over the message to Captain Stevenson.

The captain flashes a smile, happier than you have ever seen him. “Excellent news, sergeant. Gustafson reports that Rosenhain scored a great victory against the natives north of the Five Lakes. He has taken a thousand prisoners. In his honor, the emperor himself has invited us to a reception he will be hosting at the Great Velvet Hall in Quebec on February the 22nd.”

“That is only 10 days from now,” you respond.

“Then we have no time to lose. Let us make haste!”

 

 

 

 

Great Velvet Hall houses the oldest and grandest ballroom in all of North America, your father always said. Until now, you have seen only paintings of it. Yet even the finest artist could not capture the glamour and splendor of the building’s interior in its entirety.

Despite the field marshal’s best efforts, word leaked of the reception and the city militia was forced to form a barricade to keep out the crowds. Along the entire street, cordoned off by a seemingly endless line of infantry clad in resplendent blue-and-white uniforms, thousands of civilians gather hoping to catch a glimpse of the emperor. In fact, Lord Alger had arrived days ago through a side door — the same door by which you are about to enter.

You are not terribly impressed by the dimly lit corridor you initially step into; that is, until you walk through the next doorway and find yourself in the ballroom of Great Velvet Hall.

The reality before you quickly shatters several misconceptions from your upbringing:

·       Contrary to its name, the ballroom stores relatively little velvet. There is velvet coating on some of the chairs, but most of the room is colored with either gold or white oak.

·       Although it is owned by European royalty, Great Velvet Hall employs few servants. The waiters and butlers are easy to identify: their black-and-white suits create a sharp distinction from the colorful evening dress of the attendees like yourself. Yet even at an event of this scale, you can count all the servants you see on two hands.

·       The food is extravagant, but not plentiful. You had heard stories of chocolate fountains flowing thick enough to fill a river and serving plates wide enough to sleep on; however, the plate you are handed as you enter is barely large enough to fit a few slices of fruit, and anyhow many of the hors d'oeuvres are gone by the time you get to them. You make some brief conversation with those standing nearby, waiting for a waiter to refill the trays; but none does, and you are left with nothing more than a glass of red wine. You realize the menus are barely half a page long, leaving you highly suspicious that the usual lavishness and indulgence associated with the Imperial Office is lacking tonight. Perhaps the emperor is hiding something?

The rest of your surroundings temporarily divert your attention from these discrepancies. Majestic paintings of angels adorn the ceiling, from which a dozen golden chandeliers hang. Even though it is already sunset, there are so many candles on each chandelier that the room is lit as bright as if it were day. The floor tiles, lined with Ionian marble, combine to form a mosaic spanning the entire room that depicts the coronation of Innocent I, the first European emperor. The walls alternate between enormous crimson curtained windows and equally grandiose portraits of past emperors.

It is underneath the recently unveiled portrait of Emperor Alger that you see the largest group assembled. Wine in hand, you walk over to get a closer view and see three field marshals chatting heartily with a few elegantly dressed men you do not recognize: though by the echelons on their uniforms, you can tell they are colonels from other armies. Gustafson is missing. Onlookers of all types stand by, hoping to glean wisdom from the ruminations of the European military’s highest officers. Even the colonels are many ranks above your station, but having already made the field marshals’ acquaintance you expect you will be admitted cordially.

You maneuver through the crowd and attempt to join the conversation, in time to hear Rosenhain finish a joke.

“… when this painting was first completed, the nose was wider than his hand and as long as that of a pastry bird!” he says with accompanying hand gestures.

The field marshal’s companions are thrown into hysterics, laughing so fervently you feel pressured to chuckle along.

In the few seconds it takes the others to recover, you approach Rosenhain, and the expression on his face indicates he does not recall who you are.

“Marshal Rosenhain, you may not remember me. Corporal — I mean Sergeant Cameron Polagard. I used to guard the Imperial Office in Paris.”

“Of course! The emperor’s pet corporal! How could I have forgotten,” he says, offering you a firm handshake. “Thrusting yourself straight into the life of the party: you really have not changed much since we last spoke. Very well, I suppose only a fool would fix what isn’t broken.” Rosenhain proceeds to explain what transpired in the north: how he led three British armies to a spectacular victory against one of Blue Potato’s most experienced generals. He has clearly told the tale many times, but he nonetheless delivers it with alacrity and great passion. Though there is a faint sense of disdain in his voice, it is nothing more than you would predict from a field marshal speaking to a subordinate of your standing, and his overall tone is rather friendly. You get the impression that Rosenhain is the most affable individual in the Imperial Office, whose other members remain ignorant of your presence.

Your conversation lasts longer than you had expected, but eventually Rosenhain ends his story and stops asking you questions. His increasingly indifferent manner as he listens to you signals his desire to move on — he is simply too polite to leave without an invitation. You decide it is best to take the hint.

“Marshal, the news of your victory over the native hordes never ceases to move me. My best wishes for your continued success in the field,” you say.

“And the same to you,” he replies. “Forgive me, I must see to other matters, but let me introduce you to Colonel Morris of the Sixth British Army. His contribution was invaluable. Gentlemen, this is Sergeant Polagard, Third Swiss Army.” With that, the field marshal heads off to the other side of the room.

“It’s a pleasure to meet you,” you say to the colonel. “Please accept my sincerest congratulations.”

“Yes, Sergeant Polagard, you made quite a name for yourself a while back,” Colonel Morris says, declining to acknowledge your commendation. Field Marshal Kragforge, whom the colonel had been talking to, casts you a bemused look.

“Marshal Kragforge. I believe we have met,” you say to Kragforge.

“Indeed. How have you been getting along?” he asks.

You start to reveal the details of your exploits, but it is obvious that Kragforge and Colonel Morris have little interest in hearing your answer. They nod in agreement as you say a few words before respectfully excusing themselves to refill their wine glasses.

None of the other officers pay you much attention. You look around, hoping to spot a familiar face, and see Phoebe standing by herself in the corner. Her light purple dress is quite pretty, as is the way her hair curls down the side of her face. You wonder why she is alone.

She turns around when you put a hand on her shoulder. “Oh, Sergeant Polagard! I received your note, but I did not know when I might see you again.”

“Forgive me, Phoebe.” you say. “Captain Stevenson does not wish for us to be together. Hence, I had to wait until now.”

“You are forgiven. I would have sought you out, sergeant, but my brother would never let me pursue suitors. Please understand.”

“Not at all. Now then, what are you doing here by yourself?”

“I always hate parties like this,” Phoebe says. “The constricting costumes, the bland food, the stuck-up martinets who insist on addressing you by your full title. ‘It is a delight to make your acquaintance, Miss Phoebe Dunbury, Daughter to Miss Martha Dunbury, Brother to Sergeant Harris Dunbury, and Heiress to the Dunbury Estate.’ If I had a nickel for every time I heard that …”

“… you could buy Great Velvet Hall,” you say, finishing her sentence.

“Where have you been since I saw you last? Reading minds?”

“Yes,” you say. “But it works only on people with the last name Dunbury who loathe dinner parties.”

Phoebe’s laugh makes you tingle with glee. “So, what is next for you, Polagard? Surely you did not come here just to see me.”

“I am seeking a promotion,” you say. “There is only so much I can accomplish as a sergeant.”

“You know, I heard the assistant colonel was transferred to the Second Danish Army. You should consult Colonel Stanley. I believe he is still interviewing candidates.”

You ask Phoebe who has applied, but before she can answer, you hear the sound of a trumpet. The emperor is about to enter.

The emperor strolls in all smiles, basking in confidence and waving his hands toward the crowd like a politician. He wears his finest robes, a combination of red and white silks with yellow epaulets. The guards bring their hands to a salute, one after another, as he walks by.

Lord Alger’s speech is long and all-encompassing. Field Marshal Rosenhain is summoned to the podium, where the emperor awards him the Imperial Medal of Honor. He is followed by Colonel Harris, who is awarded the Star of Merit for Distinguished Service. Several other lieutenants, captains, and sergeants are called up to receive medals of their own. You are not one of them.

Indeed, you sense some grumbling among the men under your command as at least one member of almost every other regiment in the room is presented with some recognition or another by the emperor. They murmur among themselves, throwing around complaints about how the Third Swiss Army saw limited action while Rosenhain and his associates were fighting the natives. You decide to let them be and take another sip of wine. It is not until Lord Alger begins his concluding remarks that you realize Phoebe is holding your hand.

Finally, the emperor finishes speaking. As Phoebe’s carriage driver comes to escort her away, she turns to you. “Promise me I will see you soon,” she says.

“You have my word, my lady.”

 

Colonel Stanley takes off his glasses and looks you in the eyes. “Tell me why you want to be an assistant colonel.”

“There are many ways to serve,” you say. “But I did not choose to become a sergeant. The needs of the army chose me. And so here I am again, stepping up to fulfill what the force requires. Were it a staff sergeant or lieutenant, I would behave no other way. Colonel, I have seen your leadership firsthand, and I cannot say I am content resting on my laurels any longer. I must answer the call to action.”

“What about your current position?”

“I have already identified a successor, sir: Cardinal Heriot, a corporal in the first division.” In reality, you barely know Heriot beyond the fact that he is an ambitious man who would welcome a promotion.

“Assistant Colonel is a major leap for you, Sergeant Polagard. You intend to skip three ranks. In my lifetime, I can remember only a handful of times when someone achieved such a feat. Most of them did not live long afterward. And in spite of it, there are dozens of men in this army who would pay handsomely for the position you seek.”

You gulp with anxious anticipation at what the Colonel is about to say.

 “You’re hired, Polagard. Congratulations.”

 

 

 

 

Shortly after your interview with Colonel Stanley, the weather turns sour. You, as well as the rest of your regiment, are forced to lodge in Quebec for a few weeks. It is not until mid-April of 1808 that you and your men learn your next assignment: the Third Swiss Army is to embark on a journey west to the Five Lakes, and then join seven other European armies in a joint attack on Fort Toronto and the surrounding towns. Emboldened by his latest success, you presume, Field Marshal Gustafson is bringing together nearly all the troops he can muster to strike straight at the native field headquarters. It is a risky operation, but if executed properly it could very well end the war.

 

You have a hard time settling into your new role, which you soon find out is a position manufactured by Colonel Stanley for him to leave all his unwanted tasks to you. Although you report directly to the Colonel, you are frequently left out of the most important deliberations. While he discusses strategy with Captain Stevenson and Staff Sergeant Ricardo, more often than not you are stuck in your office inspecting muskets or procuring the next week’s rations. At least you now know why Field Marshal Gustafson was not at the emperor’s reception. As Assistant Colonel, you are responsible for processing the Colonel’s mail, a duty which allows you to gain many insights from his correspondence with the Imperial Office.

It turns out the massive native defeat came with a silver lining: they had been routed on the field, but as the native soldiers scattered they came upon European supply wagons like predators to prey. Isolated and insufficiently guarded, crate after crate of packed goods and ammunition were seized by the natives. The emperor was furious, and assigned Gustafson to personally restore the supply lines. The wagons were now to travel in groups of five, each protected by nine privates and a corporal.

Your rapid rise within the army brings with it both admirers and enemies. You receive an array of letters from family and friends back in Switzerland overjoyed at the news of your promotion, but your own men are not so pleased. The idea of a 23-year-old as a colonel’s second-in-command is practically unheard of, and hardly any troops take your orders seriously. One night, you return from a foraging mission only to find your tent in flames. By the time the fire is put out, all your belongings have been reduced to charred ashes. You want to hunt down your would-be murderer, but the Colonel assures you he will handle it. Not wanting to be reprimanded for disobedience, you spend your spare evenings reading up on news from Europe and spending the earnings from your increased salary on a new horse. Anyhow, you don’t have time to worry about petty grudges. Your regiment is about to enter the vicinity of the Five Lakes: native territory.

 

On the first of May, you awaken to the sound of banging drums. You step out of your tent and are greeted by a shower of arrows. Most miss, but one grazes your arm, causing you to reel back in pain. It is an early morning assault, intended to catch you by surprise.

“To arms!” you yell. “To arms! To arms!”

Staff Sergeant Ricardo blows his bugle, and the soldiers of the Third Swiss Army react immediately. You put on your helmet and unsheathe your sword. Not even bothering to put on their uniforms, your troops scramble out of their tents, grabbing the nearest weapon and bracing themselves for the oncoming charge.

Native warriors, some on foot and others on horseback, storm into your camp. One of them runs toward you with menacing eyes and an axe in each hand. The two of you parry, exchanging blows without significantly hurting the other. Suddenly, your adversary is startled by a thunderous explosion on the other side of the camp. You take advantage of his momentarily lapse to thrust your sabre into his chest.

As he falls to the ground, you recognize one of the enemy cavalrymen: Bone Wrangler, a major general in the Native American army. What is he doing here leading such a small expedition? By European standards, a skirmish of this size is fit for a lieutenant, maybe a captain, but certainly not a general. Whatever the reason, you must capture him. You reach for your musket and aim for his horse.

Your shot dismounts him. Now you rush forward, sabre at the ready. Bone Wrangler carries a sharp dagger and lunges as you approach. In the midst of blocking a blow, you hear the sound of a native war cry behind you. You glance just in time to see Sergeant Marcian tackling the native axeman. He may have just saved your life, but you cannot thank Marcian now: Bone Wrangler has pulled out a pistol, and you are his target.

His shot rips through your lower leg, and you keel over clutching at the wound. Bone Wrangler jumps onto you instantly and wrestles the sword out of your hand. You attempt to fight, but he is stronger than you are. You watch in horror helplessly as he is about to send the blade of his dagger piercing through your skull …

… only to see Bone Wrangler blinded by a kick of sand, followed by the crunch of a familiar boot. Colonel Stanley picks up your sword and, almost in slow motion, plunges it straight into the native general’s right eye. The blood splatters onto your tunic. It takes you a few moments to get yourself upright, and when you do you are greeted by the sight of Bone Wrangler being disarmed by Marcian and Bunthal. The colonel hands you your sword.

“You’re welcome, Polagard.” The noise of battle is gone, replaced by the sounds of stretchers being unfolded and by tent flaps opening. The attack has failed.

 

You can still walk, but it takes every ounce of your conscience to overcome the excruciating pain. Colonel Stanley sends you to the infirmary, with Staff Sergeant Ricardo handling your duties for the rest of the day.

After supper, you are almost asleep when you hear a chair squeaking. You open your eyes to a friendly pair of blue pupils. “I wanted to make sure you were alright.”

Phoebe’s voice is graceful and gentle, like a nurse looking after her patient. “You are really too kind,” you say weakly. “Thank you. You are the best medicine.”

She blushes and gets up from her chair. “Let me bring you some water.” Phoebe leaves the room for a minute and returns with a pitcher. She pours you a glass, before sitting down at the foot of your bed.

When she smiles at you, all of a sudden you are overcome with lustful urges. For an instant, the discomfort and fatigue vanish. You set aside the glass of water, close your eyes, and lean in for a kiss.

Instead of reciprocation, you feel an unrelenting sting as Phoebe slaps you across the check. Her face is red with anger. “What the hell is wrong with you! This is how you repay my friendship?”

“I am so sorry,” you stammer. “I did not mean —”

“I must go,” she says before running out the door.

 

The memory of Phoebe rebuffing your advances prolongs your recovery. You are confined to your bed for a solid two weeks. You try to make conversation with the other men in the infirmary, but they hold you in contempt after learning that you were almost killed by a native warrior and had to be rescued by Colonel Stanley. The only one with any sympathy for you is Bunthal, dealt a bloody cut on his face by Blood Drawer in detainment. It turns out he is a recent transfer from the Eighth Russian Army, sent west on the recommendation of his uncle, Minister Sokolov. His original last name is not even Bunthal.

The sergeant comes from a wealthy and noble upbringing, raised in the salons and gentleman’s courts of Saint Petersburg. An only child, he was taught by the finest private tutors money could buy. He was groomed to follow in the footsteps of his father and grandfather, both lawyers by trade who had served the European government with great distinction. He dreamed of one day becoming solicitor general. By pulling some strings, Sokolov was able to gain his nephew admission to the Nuremberg School of Law, the only law school in Europe.

Bunthal received poor grades, however, and dropped out in his first semester to join the military. The same year, war broke out between Europe and the Native Americans. In many ways, his story resembles yours: your parents and siblings are all merchants, and your family was privileged, if not rich. You never went without food to eat or clothes to wear. Your decision to attend military academy instead of college was born out of obligation more than necessity: you could have easily afforded to become an apprentice in the most prestigious trading firm in London or Berlin, but you chose to join the army in search of greater fulfilment.
